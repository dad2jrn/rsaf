# RSAF Terminology

Canonical definitions for terms used throughout the RSAF framework. Prefer these definitions when writing or revising RSAF chapters.

---

**Account.** The credit relationship between an issuing bank and a cardholder, comprising the credit facility, the governing agreement, and the administrative relationship that generates statements, payment processing, and customer service. Distinct from the receivable.

**Account debtor.** The person obligated on a receivable, account, chattel paper, or general intangible. In consumer credit-card litigation, the defendant is typically the account debtor.

**Account-level evidence.** Documentary evidence that specifically identifies the defendant's account or receivable and documents what occurred with that specific account — for example, an executed assignment schedule that lists the defendant's account number. Contrast with program-level evidence.

**Account-specific proof.** Competent evidence that establishes a fact about the defendant's specific account or receivable, as opposed to facts about the plaintiff's program generally.

**Assignment.** A transfer of a right — including a receivable — from an assignor to an assignee. To transfer ownership of a credit-card receivable, the governing agreements typically require execution of an assignment instrument.

**Authentication.** The process of establishing that a document is what it purports to be. Authentication is the threshold requirement for admissibility and does not establish the truth of the document's contents.

**Balance evidence.** Evidence establishing the amount owed. Requires authentication, identification of the calculation methodology, and (where applicable) tracing of the balance through any transfer or servicer change.

**Bankruptcy-remote transferor.** A special-purpose entity — typically an LLC formed by the originating bank — that purchases receivables from the bank and transfers them to an issuing trust. Structured to isolate the transferred receivables from the bank's creditors.

**Burden of proof.** The obligation of a party to produce evidence sufficient to establish a fact or element of a claim. In consumer credit-card litigation, the plaintiff bears the burden of proving every required element, including its present legal right to enforce.

**Cardmember agreement.** The contract between the issuing bank and the cardholder governing the terms of the credit-card account, including interest rates, fees, payment terms, choice-of-law provisions, and arbitration clauses.

**Chain of title.** The sequence of documented transfers linking the original holder of a right to its present claimant. For a credit-card receivable, a complete chain must be documented by account-specific evidence at each link.

**Charge-off.** An accounting event in which the servicer removes a delinquent balance from its income-producing asset records and records a loss. Charge-off does not extinguish the debt or the receivable.

**Collateral certificate.** A certificate issued to the issuing trust evidencing the trust's interest in a pool of receivables. May serve as account-level evidence if the certificate, combined with an underlying schedule, identifies the defendant's receivable.

**Default.** The cardholder's failure to make required payments or otherwise satisfy a material contractual obligation.

**Indenture.** The agreement governing the relationship between the issuing trust and the indenture trustee, setting out the terms of notes issued by the trust to investors.

**Indenture trustee.** The trustee appointed under an indenture to act on behalf of noteholders. Distinct from the owner trustee.

**Issuing trust.** A special-purpose entity — commonly a Delaware statutory trust or similar structure — that holds the transferred receivables and issues securities to investors. The trust is intended to be bankruptcy-remote.

**Ownership evidence.** Documentary evidence establishing the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce the specific receivable at issue. The highest-value category for standing analysis. Requires account-specific identification and authenticated execution.

**Owner trustee.** The trustee that holds legal title to the assets of the issuing trust on behalf of the trust beneficiaries.

**Present legal right to enforce.** The plaintiff's current entitlement to collect the specific receivable from the specific defendant. The standing element of the plaintiff's claim.

**Program-level evidence.** Documentary evidence that describes how the plaintiff's securitization program is structured and how it operates generally — for example, a prospectus or Transfer and Servicing Agreement. Program-level evidence defines the evidentiary framework but does not prove what occurred with the defendant's specific account.

**Receivable.** The right to payment arising from charges, interest, fees, or other obligations incurred on a credit-card account. A receivable can be owned, transferred, pledged, or enforced independently of the administrative account relationship.

**Receivables Purchase Agreement (RPA).** The agreement under which the originating bank sells pools of eligible receivables to the bankruptcy-remote transferor. Program-level document that may require account-specific schedules to document each transfer.

**Reacquisition theory.** The plaintiff's theory that a receivable was transferred into the securitization structure and later returned to the plaintiff through documented removal and reassignment procedures.

**Removal.** The process by which a receivable is taken out of an issuing trust pursuant to governing agreement procedures — typically upon default, charge-off, or ineligibility.

**Reassignment.** The documented return of a removed receivable from the trust or transferor to the originating bank or another designated party.

**Retention theory.** The plaintiff's theory that the specific receivable was never transferred into the securitization structure and remained with the plaintiff continuously.

**Securitization.** The process by which pools of receivables are sold to investors through a special-purpose trust, with the trust issuing securities backed by the cash flows of those receivables.

**Servicer.** The entity that administers credit-card accounts: sending statements, processing payments, maintaining records, and charging off delinquent accounts. The servicer is often the originating bank. Servicing does not confer ownership of the receivable.

**Servicing evidence.** Documents generated in the course of account administration — monthly statements, payment histories, charge-off records. Relevant to account activity and balance; not standing alone sufficient to establish ownership.

**Standing.** A party's legal right to maintain a particular action. In receivable litigation, the plaintiff's standing requires it to establish its present legal right to enforce the specific receivable at issue.

**Structural evidence.** See program-level evidence.

**Transfer and Servicing Agreement (TSA).** The agreement governing the transfer of receivables from the bankruptcy-remote transferor to the issuing trust, and the servicer's ongoing obligations. Program-level document.

**True sale.** A transfer intended to convey ownership of receivables, rather than create a secured interest in them. True-sale characterization affects whether the transferred receivables are available to the seller's creditors in bankruptcy.

**UCC Article 9.** Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code, governing secured transactions and the sale of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes. The sale of credit-card receivables falls within Article 9's scope.
