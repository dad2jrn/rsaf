## OLQ-001 — Routine Securitization Inference

**Question.** Whether Virginia law, Delaware law, or applicable federal evidence law recognizes a rebuttable evidentiary inference that a specific credit-card receivable was transferred because the issuing bank routinely transferred eligible receivables pursuant to a publicly disclosed securitization program.

**Status.** Unresolved. No binding authority in Virginia, Delaware, or the Fourth Circuit has been identified directly answering this question in the consumer collection context.

**Why It Matters.** If governing law recognizes this inference, a plaintiff may be able to establish the transfer step through routine practice evidence combined with proof of the receivable's eligibility — without producing an account-specific transfer schedule. If governing law does not recognize this inference, the plaintiff must produce account-specific documentation on the transfer element regardless of how comprehensive and well-documented the securitization program is.

The answer affects the evidentiary weight of program-level evidence (prospectuses, PSA, annual reports on EDGAR) in consumer collection litigation. It determines whether structural evidence alone can satisfy — or contribute to satisfying — the plaintiff's burden on the present-right-to-enforce element.

**Competing Positions.**

*Position A — No inference without account-specific proof.* Program documents establish how the transfer framework is structured. They do not establish what happened to any specific receivable. The plaintiff must produce account-specific evidence at each link in the chain. No governing Virginia or Fourth Circuit authority recognizes a broader inference from routine securitization practice.

*Position B — Rebuttable inference from documented routine practice.* Rules of evidence in many jurisdictions recognize that evidence of an organization's routine practice is admissible to prove that the organization acted in conformity with that practice on a particular occasion. If a bank's documented, publicly disclosed practice was to transfer all eligible receivables into the trust, and the defendant's receivable was eligible, a court could draw the inference that this receivable was transferred — subject to rebuttal by account-specific evidence of exclusion.

*Position C — SEC filings as binding party admissions.* The bank's own SEC filings describe its securitization program and the scope of the transfers made. To the extent those filings constitute party admissions, the bank may not contradict them in subsequent litigation. Whether, and how, this doctrine applies to the specific plaintiff in a consumer collection action — which may be the bank itself, a successor, or a third-party purchaser — requires additional analysis.

**Authorities Reviewed.**

- *Va. Code § 8.01-397.1 / Va. Rule of Evidence 2:406* (routine practice) — Confirmed. Enacted 2000. Applies to organizations in civil proceedings only. Defines "routine practice" as "a regular course of conduct of a group of persons or an organization in response to repeated specific situations." Corroboration not required. The Virginia Supreme Court applied the doctrine in *Kimberlin v. PM Transport, Inc.*, 264 Va. 261, 269 (2002), cautioning that examples of the conduct must be "sufficiently numerous and regular" and that the doctrine is "never to be lightly established." *Kimberlin* is a personal injury case. No Virginia appellate decision applying the doctrine to a financial institution's commercial conduct has been identified.
- *Federal Rule of Evidence 406* (routine practice of an organization) — Reviewed. Corroboration not required. No federal court opinion has been identified applying FRE 406 to create an inference that a specific consumer receivable was transferred as part of a bank's routine securitization program.
- *12 C.F.R. § 360.6* — FDIC Safe Harbor Rule (effective in current form 2020). The regulation requires securitization sponsors to "separately identify in its financial asset data bases the financial assets transferred" and to maintain "an electronic or paper copy of the closing documents for each securitization." This requirement cuts against the routine practice inference: if the bank's own regulatory framework required account-level tracking of transferred assets, those records should exist, and their absence in litigation is not bridged by a routine practice argument. See Chapter 22, §22.4.4.
- *ASC 860* (FASB Accounting Standards Codification, Transfers and Servicing) — Reviewed. A control-based derecognition standard for accounting purposes. A bank's accounting recognition of a securitization transfer as a "sale" represents to investors and regulators that it surrendered control of the transferred assets. This is an accounting standard, not an evidentiary rule, and does not independently establish the legal chain of title for a specific receivable in litigation.
- *17 C.F.R. Part 229 (Regulation AB)* — Reviewed. Governs disclosure requirements for registered ABS offerings. Requires pool-level description of transferred assets in public filings. Does not require per-account identification. Does not create a litigation presumption that any specific receivable was included in a disclosed pool.
- *6 Del. C. Ch. 27A, §§ 2701A et seq.* — Delaware Asset-Backed Securities Facilitation Act, addressing true-sale treatment under Delaware law — existence confirmed; application in Virginia litigation not yet resolved.
- *Green v. Portfolio Recovery Associates, LLC*, 80 Va.App. 119, 897 S.E.2d 275 (Va. Ct. App. 2024) (en banc) — Addressed ownership as merits element, not standing threshold. PRA relied on generic bills of sale for multiple accounts without specific account identification. The court did not address whether routine securitization practice could substitute for or supplement account-specific documentation. The routine practice inference was not presented.

**Authorities Still Needed.**

- Virginia appellate decisions applying Va. Code § 8.01-397.1 (Rule 2:406) to the conduct of a financial institution or commercial organization — none identified to date.
- Fourth Circuit opinions addressing whether program-level securitization documentation, combined with proof of eligibility, satisfies the plaintiff's burden on the ownership element in consumer collection litigation — none identified to date.
- Bankruptcy court opinions (within the Fourth Circuit or nationally) addressing the sufficiency of securitization transfer documentation in consumer receivables proof-of-claim disputes — none identified to date.
- Delaware case law on the routine practice doctrine as applied in commercial or consumer collection contexts.
- OCC guidance on documentary standards for national bank asset transfers and securitization.

**Current RSAF Position.**

RSAF currently treats public securitization documents as structural evidence defining the plaintiff's own transfer framework. RSAF does not presently treat those documents alone as proof that a specific receivable was transferred. Whether governing law permits a broader evidentiary inference remains an open legal question.

The FDIC safe harbor research strengthens the conservative default: if the bank operated a compliant securitization program, its own regulatory framework required it to separately identify transferred assets in its databases and maintain closing documents for each securitization. The absence of those records in litigation is inconsistent with the bank's own compliance obligations — not merely a gap in the defendant's counter-evidence.

**Confidence.** Low. The routine practice doctrine exists under both Virginia and federal evidence law, and its text would support the inference argument in the abstract. No binding authority applying the doctrine to securitization transfers in consumer collection litigation has been identified — for or against. RSAF's conservative default is not a conclusion that the inference is legally unavailable; it is a default pending authority.

**Research Priority.** High. This question affects the outcome of any case in which the plaintiff relies on program-level evidence without producing account-specific transfer documentation.

**Next Research Step.** Search Virginia appellate and circuit court decisions for any application of Va. Code § 8.01-397.1 to commercial or financial organizations. Search bankruptcy court opinions within the Fourth Circuit on proof-of-claim standards for securitized consumer debt. Search Fourth Circuit civil opinions for any discussion of whether pool-level assignment documentation satisfies the burden of proving ownership of a specific account.

**Cross-References.** Chapter 21 (Inference Classification), Section 21.6 and Section 21.10. Chapter 22 (Routine Securitization Practice), in full.

## OLQ-002 — Servicer Standing to Sue in Own Name

**Question.** Whether a servicer that is not the owner of the receivable has standing to commence collection litigation in its own name in Virginia, without identifying the trust or owner as the real party in interest.

**Status.** Unresolved. Green v. PRA addressed ownership as a merits element but did not squarely address whether a servicer, as distinct from an owner, may sue in its own name.

**Why It Matters.** Many collection plaintiffs are banks that continue to service charged-off receivables the bank transferred to a trust. If the bank holds the receivable as servicer but not as owner, the bank may not be the real party in interest under Virginia Rules of Civil Procedure, Rule 3:17. A judgment obtained by a servicer that does not own the receivable may be voidable.

**Competing Positions.**

*Position A — Servicer lacks standing.* Only the owner of the receivable may enforce it. A servicer acting on behalf of a trust must either (a) sue in the trust's name, (b) obtain an assignment or power of attorney from the trust, or (c) join the trust as a necessary party. Commencing suit as if the servicer were the owner when it is not misrepresents the plaintiff's legal status.

*Position B — Servicer authorized by governing agreement.* The Transfer and Servicing Agreement may expressly authorize the servicer to commence collection litigation in its own name on behalf of the trust. Courts in other jurisdictions have held that this express authorization satisfies standing requirements. The governing agreement's authorization, if established, may be sufficient.

**Authorities Reviewed.** Virginia Rule of Civil Procedure 3:17 (real party in interest). Green v. PRA (ownership as merits element; servicer standing not directly addressed).

**Authorities Still Needed.** Virginia case law on servicer authority in collection litigation. Fourth Circuit opinions. Review of the specific governing agreement's servicer-authority provisions. See Chapter 13 (Virginia Law).

**Current RSAF Position.** RSAF treats the servicer-is-not-the-owner distinction as a category error to be identified and documented. Whether a servicer has standing to sue in its own name depends on the specific governing agreement and the applicable procedural law. RSAF does not resolve this as a general rule.

**Confidence.** Low. Jurisdiction-specific and agreement-specific.

**Research Priority.** Medium. Highly fact-dependent; relevant primarily where the plaintiff's relationship to the receivable is ambiguous.

**Next Research Step.** Read the real-party-in-interest rule in Virginia (Va. R. Sup. Ct. 3:17 or equivalent). Search for Virginia cases addressing servicer authority in consumer collection actions. Read the servicer-authority provisions in the governing agreement for the specific case.

**Cross-References.** Chapter 02 (Guiding Principles), Section 2.9. Chapter 06 (Burden of Proof). Chapter 13 (Virginia Law).

## OLQ-003 — Delaware ABSFA Effect on Virginia Chain-of-Title Analysis

**Question.** Whether Delaware's Asset-Backed Securities Facilitation Act (6 Del. C. §§ 2701A et seq.) affects the chain-of-title analysis in Virginia consumer collection litigation involving receivables transferred under a Delaware-law securitization structure.

**Status.** Unresolved. Virginia courts have not directly addressed whether Delaware's ABSFA true-sale provisions alter the evidentiary analysis in Virginia.

**Why It Matters.** Delaware law governs many securitization structures (trust formation, TSA, PSA) because the issuing trust is organized under Delaware law. Delaware's ABSFA provides that a transfer qualifying under its provisions is a true sale and cannot be recharacterized as a secured financing. If ABSFA's classification is binding in Virginia, it may have consequences for the standing analysis.

**Competing Positions.**

*Position A — Delaware classification governs only in Delaware.* Virginia courts apply Virginia law to determine ownership of property in litigation before a Virginia court. Delaware's statutory characterization of a transfer as a true sale is persuasive but not binding in Virginia.

*Position B — Choice-of-law clause incorporates Delaware classification.* Where the governing agreement specifies Delaware law, Virginia courts applying the choice-of-law clause may give effect to Delaware's ABSFA characterization. This would make the true-sale classification applicable in Virginia litigation.

**Authorities Reviewed.** 6 Del. C. §§ 2701A et seq. (Delaware ABSFA). Chapter 14 (Delaware Law).

**Authorities Still Needed.** Virginia Supreme Court or Court of Appeals opinions on choice-of-law in securitization structures. Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws analysis as applied in Virginia.

**Current RSAF Position.** RSAF notes the Delaware structure and its law, but does not apply Delaware law conclusions to override Virginia evidentiary requirements. The choice-of-law analysis is case-specific and requires further research.

**Confidence.** Low.

**Research Priority.** Low. Relevant primarily in cases where the plaintiff invokes Delaware law as a defense to chain-of-title challenges.

**Next Research Step.** Research Virginia choice-of-law doctrine (Restatement (Second) of Conflict of Laws as applied in Virginia) to determine whether Delaware's statutory true-sale characterization follows the transferred asset into Virginia litigation. Identify any Virginia cases where a Delaware-law true-sale argument was raised in a consumer collection action.

**Cross-References.** Chapter 14 (Delaware Law). Chapter 15 (Federal Law).
