# 21 — Inference Classification

## 21.1 Purpose

RSAF must distinguish facts from inferences. This is not a formality. The distinction determines what conclusions RSAF may draw from the evidentiary record, what assertions require qualification, and what uncertainties must be disclosed rather than resolved by assumption.

In consumer credit-card receivable litigation, the record frequently contains a mixture of proven facts, reasonable inferences from documentary evidence, legal inferences required by governing law, alternative explanations that the evidence does not rule out, legal questions without settled answers, and assertions that are simply unsupported. These categories are not interchangeable. Treating an inference as a proven fact — or treating speculation as a reasonable inference — is an analytical error with potential consequences in court.

This chapter defines each category, explains its purpose in RSAF analysis, provides examples drawn from credit-card securitization, identifies common mistakes, and addresses the judicial significance of each distinction.

---

## 21.2 Category 1: Proven Fact

**Definition.** A proven fact is a proposition established by competent, admissible evidence in the record. It does not require inference, assumption, or extrapolation. The evidence directly establishes the proposition.

**Purpose.** Proven facts form the foundation of the analysis. Every element of a legal claim must ultimately rest on proven facts if it is to be established. RSAF's task is to identify which facts are actually proven and which are being asserted without adequate support.

**Examples in credit-card securitization.**

- The defendant applied for and received a credit card from [Bank], as established by the cardmember agreement and account-opening records in evidence.
- The account had an outstanding balance of $[X] as of [date], as established by an authenticated account statement generated by the servicer.
- The account was charged off on [date], as established by an authenticated charge-off record.
- Capital One Funding, LLC is identified in the governing Pooling and Servicing Agreement as the transferor entity, as established by the PSA itself.
- The plaintiff purchased a portfolio of accounts pursuant to a bill of sale executed on [date], as established by the authenticated bill of sale.

**Common mistakes.**

The most common error is treating an assertion in a custodian affidavit as a proven fact without asking whether the assertion is supported by underlying documents in evidence. A custodian who testifies that "plaintiff owns this account" has made an assertion. That assertion becomes a proven fact only if it is supported by admissible documents establishing the chain of title. The assertion is not independently a proven fact.

**Judicial significance.** Courts draw a consistent distinction between testimonial assertions about legal conclusions and documentary evidence establishing the underlying facts. A judge evaluating ownership will look past the characterization to the underlying documents. RSAF must identify whether there are underlying documents that support the assertion, not merely that the assertion was made.

## 21.3 Category 2: Supported Documentary Inference

**Definition.** A supported documentary inference is a conclusion that is not directly stated by any single document but that follows reasonably from the combination of documents in evidence, under a standard of reasonableness that a court could accept.

**Purpose.** Many facts relevant to standing are not directly stated in any single document. An inference is necessary and appropriate where: (a) there are multiple authenticated documents; (b) the inference follows logically from those documents; and (c) the inference is not contradicted by other evidence. RSAF may draw and present such inferences, but must identify them as inferences, not proven facts.

**Examples in credit-card securitization.**

- The executed Transfer and Servicing Agreement, the UCC-1 financing statement identifying [Bank] as debtor and [Trust] as secured party, and the trust's 10-K annual report together support the inference that the bank established and maintained an active securitization program under which eligible receivables were sold to the trust.
- The account was opened in [year], the bank's prospectus for the [trust series] indicates that eligible accounts opened after [date] were included in the trust's receivable pool, and no exclusion documentation has been produced — these facts together support the inference that this account met the eligibility criteria described in the program documents. (Note: this is a supported inference about eligibility, not proof of actual transfer.)
- The removal and reassignment provisions in the PSA require an executed removal notice and a reassignment schedule — the plaintiff's failure to produce either, after demand, supports the inference that those documents do not exist or are not in the plaintiff's possession.

**Common mistakes.**

A supported documentary inference must rest on authenticated documents, not on assertions or summaries. An inference that a bank "always transferred" eligible receivables based on the existence of a securitization program is an overstated inference — it states more than the documents actually show. Similarly, the absence of a document in the produced record supports a more limited inference (that the document has not been produced) rather than the broader inference that the underlying event did or did not occur.

**Judicial significance.** Courts accept reasonable documentary inferences, especially in summary judgment and trial practice. However, an inference presented as a proven fact may draw objection, and a court that evaluates the inference may find it does not carry the weight assigned to it. RSAF must present supported documentary inferences with appropriate qualification, distinguishing them from proven facts in the analysis.

## 21.4 Category 3: Legal Inference

**Definition.** A legal inference is a conclusion that governing law requires a court to draw from a proven fact, or that a statute or rule makes available to a party upon proof of certain predicate facts.

**Purpose.** Legal inferences are not the same as evidentiary inferences. A legal inference is not drawn from evidence by a reasoning process; it is assigned by law. When a statute or rule says that proof of fact A creates a presumption of fact B, that is a legal inference. RSAF must distinguish legal inferences from factual reasoning.

**Examples in credit-card securitization.**

- Under UCC Article 9 (both Virginia § 8.9A-109 and Delaware 6 Del. C. § 9-109), the sale of accounts is treated as a transfer of ownership for purposes of Article 9's priority rules — this is a legal characterization that does not depend on the parties' characterization of the transaction.
- Where a party-to-a-contract sues for breach, certain procedural presumptions about the validity of the contract may arise, depending on the forum's procedural law. These are legal inferences rather than inferences from documentary evidence.
- Under applicable rules of evidence, an authenticated business record may be presumed reliable within the scope of the business-records exception — the presumption is a legal inference that shifts the burden on the question of admissibility.

**Common mistakes.**

A legal inference is only available if the predicate facts required by the governing law have been proven. RSAF must identify what predicate is required before citing a legal inference. A legal inference is also jurisdiction-specific. RSAF must not apply a legal inference available in one jurisdiction to a matter governed by another.

**Judicial significance.** Legal inferences are important because they can shift the burden or reduce the quantum of proof required on a specific element. However, they are not substitutes for the underlying predicate proof. A court will first ask whether the predicate has been established before accepting the legal inference.

## 21.5 Category 4: Competing Explanation

**Definition.** A competing explanation is an alternative account of the facts that is consistent with the available evidence but different from the inference the plaintiff draws from that same evidence.

**Purpose.** RSAF must evaluate whether the plaintiff's evidence supports only one reasonable interpretation or whether the same evidence is consistent with an explanation that does not establish the plaintiff's claim. Where a competing explanation exists and is supported by the evidence, the evidence is ambiguous. Ambiguous evidence does not establish the plaintiff's burden.

**Examples in credit-card securitization.**

- Monthly account statements bearing the originating bank's name are consistent with two explanations: (a) the bank is the owner of the receivable; and (b) the bank is the servicer acting on behalf of a trust that holds the receivable. The statements alone do not resolve this ambiguity.
- A bill of sale transferring "all charged-off accounts listed on Schedule A" without producing Schedule A is consistent with both (a) the account being included in the transfer and (b) the account not being on the schedule.
- A custodian affidavit describing the plaintiff's records and asserting that the plaintiff owns the account is consistent with both (a) that assertion being accurate and (b) the affiant describing the plaintiff's own characterization, not the underlying documentary chain.

**Common mistakes.**

RSAF must not present a competing explanation as proof that the plaintiff's theory is wrong. A competing explanation demonstrates that the evidence is ambiguous — it does not prove the competing explanation is correct. The burden remains with the plaintiff to resolve the ambiguity by producing evidence that distinguishes the alternative interpretation.

RSAF must also not manufacture far-fetched competing explanations. An alternative that is inconsistent with the evidence in the record is speculation, not a competing explanation. Competing explanations must themselves be grounded in the evidence.

**Judicial significance.** Where competing explanations exist, the plaintiff has not met its burden by producing evidence that is merely consistent with its theory. The evidence must be sufficient to establish the theory, not merely consistent with it. A court evaluating sufficiency of evidence will consider whether the plaintiff's evidence excludes reasonable alternative explanations that would defeat the claim.

## 21.6 Category 5: Open Legal Question

**Definition.** An open legal question is an issue on which governing law is unclear, contested, or undecided by binding authority. An open legal question is not a gap in the evidence; it is a gap in the law. RSAF must identify open legal questions and refrain from presenting contested legal conclusions as settled law.

**Purpose.** Intellectual honesty requires acknowledging when the law does not provide a clear answer. Presenting a contested legal position as settled overstates the authority and exposes RSAF's analysis to credible challenge.

**Examples in credit-card securitization.**

- Whether Virginia law recognizes an evidentiary inference that a specific credit-card receivable was transferred because the issuing bank routinely transferred eligible receivables pursuant to a publicly disclosed securitization program. See Chapter 22 and OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md.
- Whether a servicer's authority to commence collection litigation in its own name, without identifying the trust as principal, satisfies the real-party-in-interest requirement in Virginia.
- Whether Delaware law's true-sale provisions under the Asset-Backed Securities Facilitation Act affect the chain-of-title analysis in Virginia litigation.
- Whether the FDIC Safe Harbor Rule, which codifies conditions for true-sale treatment, affects the evidentiary analysis of transfer documentation in a state court collection action.

**Common mistakes.**

The most dangerous mistake is treating an open legal question as if it were settled, because the argument is useful to the current analysis. RSAF must identify open legal questions as such, note what authority exists on each side, and state what additional research or authority would be needed to resolve the question.

**Judicial significance.** Open legal questions belong in the research layer of the analysis, not in the conclusion. A court-facing document that presents an unresolved legal question as settled law may be challenged, and the challenge may undermine the credibility of the analysis as a whole. Identifying the open question accurately and honestly is more useful to a court than asserting a confident but contestable position.

---

## 21.7 Category 6: Speculation

**Definition.** Speculation is an assertion, conclusion, or characterization that is not supported by authenticated evidence, is not a reasonable inference from the documented record, and is not required by law. It reflects assumption rather than analysis.

**Purpose.** Speculation is the one category that RSAF must eliminate from its analysis entirely. Speculative assertions are not useful to a court, expose the analysis to facile rebuttal, and undermine the credibility of the methodology.

**Examples in credit-card securitization.**

Speculation:
- "The receivable was undoubtedly transferred to the trust because that is how the program works."
- "The plaintiff cannot own this receivable because all banks sell their charged-off accounts."
- "The defendant's account was certainly removed from the trust after charge-off because that is standard practice."

Each of these assertions goes beyond the evidence in the record and beyond what governing law requires a court to conclude. They reflect assumptions about industry practice applied as facts in a specific case.

Contrast with supported inference:
- "Plaintiff's program documents indicate that eligible receivables are routinely transferred. Plaintiff has not produced account-specific evidence showing this receivable was included in a transfer or excluded from the program. Under plaintiff's burden, the absence of such evidence is an evidentiary gap on the standing element."

The second formulation stays within the evidence and within RSAF's analytical authority.

**Common mistakes.**

Speculation often enters analysis through phrases like "clearly," "obviously," "certainly," "it is evident that," or "common sense suggests." These phrases are signals that the analysis has moved beyond the evidence. RSAF must substitute: "the record establishes" (for proven facts), "the record supports the inference" (for documentary inferences), or "the record does not establish" (for gaps).

**Judicial significance.** Speculative assertions in court-facing documents undermine credibility. A judge who notices that an assertion is speculation rather than analysis will discount the work product as a whole. Accurate characterization of the evidentiary record — including its gaps and ambiguities — is more persuasive than overstated conclusions.

## 21.8 The Hierarchy of Inference and Evidence

The categories above can be organized into a rough hierarchy from strongest to weakest:

```text
Proven Fact
(established by authenticated, admissible evidence)
            ↓
Legal Inference
(assigned by governing law upon proof of predicate facts)
            ↓
Supported Documentary Inference
(reasonable conclusion from multiple authenticated documents)
            ↓
Competing Explanation
(alternative consistent with the same evidence — signals ambiguity)
            ↓
Open Legal Question
(legal uncertainty not yet resolved by binding authority)
            ↓
Speculation
(assertion without evidentiary or legal foundation — excluded from analysis)
```

RSAF must identify where each proposition in its analysis falls within this hierarchy. The burden of proof in a civil action requires the plaintiff to prove its claim by a preponderance of the evidence. A proposition that rests only on a documentary inference — even a supported one — may not carry the weight required to satisfy that burden on a critical element without additional corroborating evidence.

## 21.9 Applying Inference Classification in Practice

**Step 1.** Review each factual proposition in the analysis. Ask: Is this a proven fact, or is it an inference? If it is an inference, which kind?

**Step 2.** For each inference, identify what additional evidence would convert it to a proven fact. That additional evidence is either part of the required proof or identifies an evidentiary gap.

**Step 3.** For competing explanations: identify what the plaintiff has or has not done to foreclose them. Where a competing explanation has not been foreclosed by the evidence, the evidence is ambiguous on the relevant point.

**Step 4.** For open legal questions: identify the question in the analysis and do not present it as settled. Note what authority exists, what authority is needed, and what the current RSAF position is pending further research.

**Step 5.** Eliminate speculation from the analysis. Replace speculative assertions with accurate descriptions of the evidentiary record.

## 21.10 Structural Evidence Is Not Transaction Evidence — The Inference It Supports

A recurring inference question in RSAF analysis involves structural evidence: what inference, if any, does the existence of a securitization program support about what happened to a specific receivable?

The current RSAF position is that structural evidence supports a supported documentary inference that a transfer program exists and operates in the described manner, but does not support a proven fact that any specific receivable was transferred through it. The gap between "this program transfers eligible receivables" and "this receivable was transferred" remains an evidentiary gap until filled by account-specific documentary evidence.

Whether governing law recognizes a stronger inference — for example, a rebuttable presumption that eligible receivables were transferred under a publicly disclosed routine practice — is an open legal question. See Chapter 22 (Routine Securitization Practice) and OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md.

RSAF currently treats the structural evidence as establishing what the plaintiff's own framework requires, not as establishing that the framework was followed for this account.

## 21.11 Common Analytical Errors Involving Inference

**Error 1: Treating a custodian's assertion as a proven fact.**

A records custodian may assert, under oath, that the plaintiff owns the receivable. That assertion is not independently a proven fact on the ownership element. The custodian's assertion is only as strong as the underlying documents the assertion is based on. If the underlying documents do not establish ownership, the assertion does not either.

**Error 2: Using program-level documents to prove account-specific events.**

The structural evidence establishes what is supposed to happen under the program. It does not establish what actually happened to this receivable. The inference from structural evidence to a specific account event is a supported documentary inference at best, not a proven fact.

**Error 3: Treating industry practice as account-specific evidence.**

The fact that banks routinely transfer receivables, or routinely remove charged-off accounts from trusts, or routinely prepare removal schedules — none of these facts establishes what occurred with the defendant's specific receivable. Industry practice is background context. It does not substitute for account-specific documentation.

**Error 4: Resolving ambiguity in the plaintiff's favor without evidence.**

Where the evidence is consistent with more than one explanation, the ambiguity must be identified, not resolved in the plaintiff's favor. Resolving an ambiguity in the plaintiff's favor without evidence shifts the burden of proof. The plaintiff must produce evidence that resolves the ambiguity; RSAF does not resolve it for the plaintiff.

**Error 5: Treating an unresolved legal question as a settled legal inference.**

An open legal question about whether governing law recognizes a particular inference is not itself that inference. Until the question is resolved by binding authority, RSAF must treat it as open and must not apply it as if it were settled.
