# 06 — Burden of Proof

## 6.1 Core Rule

The plaintiff bears the burden of proving every required element of its claim. That burden does not shift to the defendant because the defendant denies an allegation, challenges the sufficiency of the plaintiff's evidence, or invokes the plaintiff's own governing documents.

This rule reflects the fundamental structure of civil litigation: the party seeking judgment must prove its entitlement to that judgment. A plaintiff that cannot prove an element of its claim is not entitled to prevail on that claim. RSAF must not obscure this principle.

---

## 6.2 Standing as an Element — and the Merits Framing Distinction

Where standing or ownership is contested, the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce the receivable is a required element that must be proven by competent evidence.

RSAF treats standing as an evidentiary question. The question is not whether a receivable once existed, whether a balance is owed, or whether statements were generated. The question is whether the plaintiff has proven, through competent admissible evidence, that it presently holds the legal right to collect this specific balance from this specific defendant.

When standing is challenged, the plaintiff must produce account-specific evidence sufficient to satisfy the governing standard. The defendant is not required to prove the plaintiff lacks standing. The defendant may challenge the sufficiency of the plaintiff's proof on this element without bearing any affirmative burden of its own.

**The standing/merits distinction — a critical framing issue.**

Virginia courts may treat proof of ownership either as a threshold standing question or as a merits element of the claim. The framing matters procedurally.

A pure standing challenge — arguing that the plaintiff has no right to be in court — may fail at the pleading stage or on summary judgment if the plaintiff can show that a genuine factual dispute exists about ownership. Where the plaintiff produces any documentation of a transfer, the court may find that the disputed factual question must go to the factfinder, defeating a pre-trial standing challenge.

A failure-of-proof challenge — arguing that the plaintiff did not carry its burden on the ownership element at trial — is not defeated by the existence of a factual dispute. It requires the plaintiff to prove its ownership case with competent, admissible, account-specific evidence at the evidentiary stage. If the plaintiff's evidence is insufficient, it loses on the merits.

**RSAF practice rule:** Both framings should be preserved and advanced. The standing challenge is appropriate and should be raised at the pleading stage and on summary judgment. The failure-of-proof challenge should be developed in parallel, framed as: the plaintiff failed to carry its burden on the ownership element because it did not produce account-specific evidence establishing an unbroken chain of title from the originating bank to the plaintiff. See Chapter 12 and Chapter 18 for case-law context on this distinction in Virginia.

## 6.3 Claim-by-Claim Burden Analysis

The elements of the plaintiff's burden depend on the theory of recovery asserted. RSAF must identify each claim and the elements the plaintiff must prove under each.

### 6.3.1 Breach of Contract

Under a breach of contract theory, the plaintiff must generally establish:

1. the existence of a valid, enforceable contract;
2. that the defendant is a party to or bound by the contract;
3. that the plaintiff has performed, or has a valid excuse for non-performance, of its obligations;
4. that the defendant breached a contractual obligation — typically by failing to pay amounts owed;
5. that the breach caused damages;
6. the amount of those damages;
7. the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce the contract.

Each element requires proof. The contract element requires producing the governing agreement in admissible form. The breach element requires admissible evidence that charges were incurred and payment was not made. The damages element requires a properly authenticated balance supported by admissible records. The standing element requires proof that the plaintiff currently holds the right to enforce — which, where ownership is contested, requires account-specific evidence of an unbroken chain of title.

### 6.3.2 Account Stated

An account stated is an agreement, express or implied, between parties who have had prior dealings, that an account rendered to the defendant is correct and that the stated amount is owed.

To prevail on an account stated theory, the plaintiff must generally establish:

1. prior transactions between the parties;
2. that an account was rendered to the defendant;
3. that the defendant acknowledged the account as correct — expressly or by retaining the statement without objection for a reasonable time;
4. that the stated amount is owed;
5. the plaintiff's present right to collect that amount.

Account stated does not eliminate the standing requirement. A plaintiff that cannot establish its right to enforce the receivable cannot recover under an account stated theory on the strength of statements alone, even if those statements were sent and not disputed.

### 6.3.3 Unjust Enrichment

Unjust enrichment typically requires the plaintiff to establish:

1. that the defendant received a benefit at the plaintiff's expense;
2. that the defendant's retention of that benefit is unjust;
3. that the plaintiff is entitled to restitution in the amount of the benefit.

Unjust enrichment is generally unavailable where a valid contract governs the parties' relationship. Where a cardmember agreement covers the transaction at issue, the plaintiff should ordinarily proceed under contract theory. Where unjust enrichment is properly asserted, the plaintiff must still establish its present right to seek the remedy.

### 6.3.4 Open Account

An open account claim is based on a running account between the parties reflecting periodic transactions arising from a continuing relationship.

The plaintiff must establish the existence of the account, the transactions comprising the balance, and its present right to collect. The same standing requirement applies.

## 6.4 The Standing Element in Detail

Standing — the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce the specific receivable — is the element most frequently at issue in consumer credit-card collection litigation involving securitized receivables.

**Standing must be proven, not assumed.** The plaintiff's decision to file suit does not establish its right to do so. The filing of a complaint is not evidence of the right to enforce.

**The required proof depends on the plaintiff's theory.**

If the plaintiff contends it has always owned the receivable and never transferred it, it must produce evidence supporting retention. The plaintiff may need to show absence of any transfer schedule, exclusion from any securitization program, or other account-specific evidence that the receivable remained with the originator throughout the relevant period.

If the plaintiff contends it acquired the receivable through assignment or reacquisition, it must produce the complete chain of title from the prior holder to itself. Each link in that chain must be documented by account-specific evidence.

**The required evidence is account-specific.** Program-level documents establish the framework. They do not prove what occurred with the defendant's receivable.

**When standing is challenged, the plaintiff must respond.** A challenge to the sufficiency of standing evidence places the burden squarely on the plaintiff to demonstrate that the evidentiary record satisfies its burden. The defendant's challenge is not itself evidence; it is an argument about the sufficiency of the plaintiff's proof. The plaintiff must answer with evidence.

---

## 6.5 Defendant's Role

The defendant in a consumer credit-card collection action occupies a specific legal position under RSAF.

The defendant may:

- challenge the sufficiency of the plaintiff's proof on any element of any claim;
- use the plaintiff's own public filings and governing agreements to identify what account-specific proof should exist and demonstrate that it has not been produced;
- raise affirmative defenses that are supported by the factual record and applicable law;
- demand production of documents that would prove or disprove the plaintiff's claimed chain of title;
- argue that the plaintiff has not met its burden on any element.

The defendant does not:

- bear the burden of proving the plaintiff lacks standing;
- bear the burden of proving that any particular transfer occurred or did not occur;
- bear the burden of disproving any element of the plaintiff's claim.

RSAF must not, in its analysis or in any document it produces, assign to the defendant the burden of proving a negative on any element of the plaintiff's claim.

## 6.6 Burden Matrix

| Element | Burden Holder | Evidence Category | Evidentiary Question |
|---|---|---|---|
| Jurisdiction | Plaintiff | Jurisdictional proof | Has the plaintiff established the basis for the court's authority over the claim and the parties? |
| Contract formation | Plaintiff | Contract documents | Is there admissible evidence of a binding agreement? |
| Defendant bound by contract | Plaintiff | Contract + identity evidence | Did the defendant agree to, or become bound by, the terms of the governing agreement? |
| Default / failure to pay | Plaintiff | Servicing evidence | Does competent evidence establish non-payment by the defendant? |
| Amount owed | Plaintiff | Balance evidence | Is the claimed amount supported by authenticated, admissible records? |
| Interest (if claimed) | Plaintiff | Contract + balance evidence | Is there a contractual or statutory basis for the rate applied, and is the calculation supported? |
| Standing / present right to enforce | Plaintiff | Ownership evidence | Has the plaintiff produced account-specific evidence of its present legal right to enforce? |
| Complete chain of title (if claimed) | Plaintiff | Transaction evidence | Is every link in the asserted transfer chain documented by account-specific evidence? |
| Attorneys' fees (if claimed) | Plaintiff | Contract + evidence | Is the fee claim supported by the governing agreement and competent proof of the amount? |

## 6.7 No Negative-Proof Requirement

RSAF must not require the defendant to prove a negative.

The burden is never: "The defendant has not shown that the plaintiff lacks standing."

The correct formulation is: "The plaintiff has not produced account-specific evidence establishing its present right to enforce this receivable."

The first formulation inverts the burden. The second correctly describes the evidentiary record without shifting any burden to the defendant.

Similarly, RSAF must not state: "The defendant must show that the receivable was transferred." Whether and how the receivable was transferred is part of the plaintiff's burden to prove, not the defendant's burden to disprove.

---

## 6.8 Evidentiary Gap Analysis

When a required element is not supported by competent evidence, RSAF must state the gap with precision.

A gap statement must address three things:

1. **What is missing.** Identify the specific document or category of evidence that the plaintiff has not produced.
2. **Why it is required.** Explain why the missing evidence is required — whether because the governing agreements demand it, applicable law requires it, or the plaintiff's asserted chain of title depends on it.
3. **What follows.** State the legal consequence under the plaintiff's burden: if the element is unproven, the plaintiff has not satisfied its burden on that element.

**Correct formulation:**

> Plaintiff has produced account statements and a records custodian affidavit. Plaintiff has not produced account-specific transfer documentation establishing how it acquired the right to enforce this receivable. Under the plaintiff's burden of proof, the absence of such evidence means the standing element remains unestablished on the current record.

**Incorrect formulation:**

> Because no assignment was produced, the plaintiff does not own the receivable.

The first describes the record and applies the burden. The second reaches a factual conclusion that requires a judicial determination — one that RSAF cannot and should not make.

---

## 6.9 Burden at Different Procedural Stages

The burden of proof does not change as a case progresses. What changes is the procedural standard the evidence is measured against.

**Pleading stage.** The plaintiff must allege facts sufficient to state a plausible claim. The court accepts well-pleaded factual allegations as true for purposes of testing the complaint. Standing challenges at this stage are directed to the face of the complaint.

**Summary judgment.** The plaintiff must establish that no genuine dispute of material fact exists and that it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law on the evidentiary record. A plaintiff that cannot produce admissible evidence sufficient to support each element of its claim cannot survive summary judgment on that element.

**Trial or arbitration.** The plaintiff must prove each element by the applicable standard — ordinarily a preponderance of the evidence in a civil collection action. At trial, the evidentiary rules apply in full.

RSAF must calibrate its analysis to the procedural stage. An analysis of whether a complaint states a claim is materially different from an analysis of whether the evidentiary record supports summary judgment, which is again different from evaluating the sufficiency of proof at trial. Each stage requires its own assessment.
