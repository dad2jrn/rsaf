# 16 — Documentary Chain of Title

## 16.1 Purpose

A chain of title is a sequence of documented transfers, each linking one owner to the next, from the original holder of a right to its present claimant. In consumer receivable litigation, the plaintiff's chain of title is the evidentiary record that connects the originating bank to the entity suing to collect.

This chapter explains what a complete chain of title looks like for a credit-card receivable, how to evaluate the chain, and what consequences flow from a missing or broken link.

---

## 16.2 What Chain of Title Means for a Receivable

For real property, chain of title is a sequence of recorded deeds. For a promissory note, it may be a sequence of endorsements. For a credit-card receivable — a contractual right to payment — the chain is a sequence of documented transfers, each of which must:

1. identify the specific receivable being transferred;
2. identify the transferor and the transferee;
3. be authorized by and consistent with the governing agreements;
4. be executed by a party with authority to transfer;
5. be authenticated by competent evidence.

Every link in the chain must satisfy these requirements. A chain is only as strong as its weakest link. A single missing or deficient link breaks the chain.

---

## 16.3 The Structural Chain vs. the Account-Specific Chain

There are two distinct levels at which a chain of title may be described.

**The structural chain** is the program-level description of how transfers ordinarily occur under the governing agreements. It identifies the participants, the agreements, and the general path:

```text
Originating Bank
        │ Receivables Purchase Agreement
        ▼
Bankruptcy-Remote Transferor
        │ Transfer and Servicing Agreement
        ▼
Issuing Trust
        │ Governing agreement procedures for removal and reassignment
        ▼
Transferor (upon removal)
        │ Executed reassignment
        ▼
Originating Bank or Third-Party Purchaser
```

The structural chain describes what the documents contemplate, not what occurred. It is established by program-level evidence.

**The account-specific chain** is the concrete record of what actually occurred with the defendant's receivable. It must be proven by account-level evidence — executed instruments and schedules that specifically identify the defendant's account or receivable at each stage of the transfer.

A plaintiff who produces only the structural chain has proven how the program works, not that this receivable traveled through it.

---

## 16.4 Evaluating Each Link

RSAF evaluates each link in the plaintiff's chain of title against the following criteria.

**Link 1: Initial Transfer (Bank to Transferor)**

- Is there an executed RPA covering the period and the receivable?
- Does an account schedule or other account-level document identify the defendant's account as included in the transfer?
- Is the RPA authenticated?
- Is the schedule, if one exists, authenticated and connected to the executed RPA?

**Link 2: Transfer to Trust (Transferor to Issuing Trust)**

- Is there an executed TSA or PSA?
- Does an account schedule or collateral certificate identify the defendant's receivable as included in the trust?
- Is the documentation authenticated?

**Link 3: Removal from Trust (if applicable)**

- What provision of the governing agreements authorized removal?
- Is there a removal notice, removal schedule, or other account-specific document evidencing removal?
- Does it identify the defendant's account?
- Is it executed by the proper party and authenticated?

**Link 4: Reassignment from Trust (if applicable)**

- Is there an executed reassignment instrument?
- Does it identify the defendant's account or reference a schedule that does?
- To whom was the receivable reassigned?

**Link 5: Any Subsequent Transfer to Current Plaintiff (if applicable)**

- If the current plaintiff is not the entity that received the reassigned receivable, what document conveys the receivable from that entity to the plaintiff?
- Does that document identify this specific receivable?
- Is it executed and authenticated?

---

## 16.5 Complete vs. Incomplete Chains

**A complete chain** provides account-specific documentation for every transfer from the originating bank to the current plaintiff. Each link is supported by an executed instrument, an authenticating foundation, and an identification of the specific receivable.

**A partial chain** is missing one or more links. The typical deficiencies are:

- Transfer into trust documented by program-level materials only, without an account-specific schedule identifying the defendant's receivable.
- Removal from trust asserted but not supported by account-level removal documentation.
- Reassignment from trust to bank described in program-level documents but not evidenced by an account-specific executed reassignment.
- A gap between the entity that originally received the reassigned receivable and the entity now suing.

**A broken chain** is one in which there is a logical inconsistency — for example, a plaintiff asserting it obtained a receivable by assignment from Entity B, but Entity B's right to assign depends on a prior transfer that is not documented and may not have occurred.

**A missing chain** is one in which the plaintiff has produced no account-specific ownership documentation at all, relying instead on servicing records and program-level materials.

---

## 16.6 What RSAF Does Not Do

**RSAF does not fill gaps in the chain.** If a link is missing, RSAF does not infer that the transfer occurred. The plaintiff bears the burden of producing evidence for every link. An inference that a transfer occurred because the program contemplates such transfers is not account-specific proof that it did.

**RSAF does not accept program-level documents as account-specific proof.** A TSA that governs how transfers occur does not prove that this receivable was transferred under it without an account schedule or other account-specific evidence.

**RSAF does not declare chain of title broken based on inference.** If the plaintiff has not produced evidence of a particular link, RSAF states the evidentiary gap. It does not reach the factual conclusion that the transfer did not occur. The conclusion is a legal determination the court must make.

---

## 16.7 Common Chain-of-Title Deficiencies

The following deficiencies recur in consumer credit-card collection litigation.

**1. Account schedule not produced.** The plaintiff produces an executed assignment instrument but does not produce the account schedule that would identify the defendant's specific account as included in the transfer. Without the schedule, the instrument does not prove this receivable was transferred.

**2. Schedule not linked to instrument.** The plaintiff produces an account schedule listing account numbers and a separately produced assignment instrument, but neither document references the other. Whether they relate to the same transfer is unclear.

**3. Authentication deficiency.** The plaintiff produces documents that purport to show a transfer but does not provide a competent foundation witness who can authenticate them as business records of the transferring entity.

**4. No removal documentation.** The plaintiff asserts the receivable was removed from the trust but produces no account-specific removal notice, removal schedule, or trustee documentation evidencing the removal.

**5. No reassignment instrument.** The plaintiff asserts that the receivable was returned after removal but produces no executed reassignment document identifying the defendant's account.

**6. Gap in the chain to current plaintiff.** The plaintiff is a successor to the entity that originally received the reassigned receivable, but the transfer from that intermediate entity to the current plaintiff is not documented.

**7. Sole reliance on affidavit.** The plaintiff offers an affidavit from a records custodian that asserts, without supporting documentation, that the plaintiff owns the receivable. An assertion of ownership in an affidavit is not a substitute for the underlying transfer documents.

---

## 16.8 Applying Chain-of-Title Analysis

RSAF's chain-of-title analysis proceeds as follows.

1. **Identify the plaintiff's theory** — retention or reacquisition.
2. **Map the structural chain** using the plaintiff's program-level documents to identify the expected transfer path and the documentation that should exist at each node.
3. **List the account-specific evidence produced** for each link in the chain.
4. **Evaluate each link** against the criteria in Section 16.4.
5. **Identify the gaps** — links supported only by structural evidence, links with authentication deficiencies, and links with no evidence at all.
6. **State the consequence** — under the plaintiff's burden, an unproven link means the chain of title is not established on the current record.

The chain-of-title analysis feeds directly into the burden assessment in Chapter 05. A plaintiff that cannot produce a complete, authenticated, account-specific chain has not satisfied its burden on the standing element.
