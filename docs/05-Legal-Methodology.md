# 05 — Legal Methodology

## 5.1 Purpose

This chapter defines RSAF's reasoning sequence.

Every RSAF analysis follows the same disciplined progression. The sequence is not optional. Skipping a step introduces the risk of reaching a legal conclusion before the evidentiary and legal foundation has been established. That risk is unacceptable in litigation.

The methodology applies regardless of case stage. Whether the matter is at the pleading stage, discovery, motion practice, arbitration, or trial, the analytical steps remain constant. The form of the work product changes; the reasoning sequence does not.

---

## 5.2 Analysis Pipeline

```text
Determine procedural posture
            ↓
Identify claims and requested relief
            ↓
Determine governing law
            ↓
Identify plaintiff's burden
            ↓
Identify governing contractual documents
            ↓
Inventory plaintiff's evidence
            ↓
Classify evidence by type
            ↓
Assess evidence weight
            ↓
Consider alternate explanations
            ↓
Identify Plaintiff's Enforcement Theory
            ↓
Map the receivable transfer path
            ↓
Identify required account-level proof
            ↓
Compare competing theories
            ↓
Compare required proof to evidence produced
            ↓
Identify evidentiary deficiencies
            ↓
Research controlling authority
            ↓
Assess whether plaintiff met its burden
            ↓
Draft requested work product, if any
```

Two framework tools structure the middle stages of analysis.

The **Required Proof Matrix** maps each element of the plaintiff's claim to the category of evidence required to establish it. It is populated after the governing law and contractual documents have been identified and serves as the evidentiary standard against which the plaintiff's actual production is measured.

The **Evidence Classification Matrix** organizes the plaintiff's produced documents by type, source, and evidentiary function. It separates structural evidence from transaction evidence, identifies gaps, and records authentication status and admissibility concerns for each document.

Both tools are analytical instruments, not conclusions. They structure the comparison between what is required and what exists.

Each step of the pipeline is explained in the sections below.

---

## 5.3 Procedural Posture

RSAF must know where the case stands before beginning analysis.

The procedural posture determines:

- the governing standard (notice pleading, plausibility, sufficiency, clear and convincing evidence, preponderance);
- what must be alleged versus what must be proven;
- what evidence is before the court;
- what defenses or arguments are available;
- what the work product must accomplish.

The four most common postures in consumer credit-card litigation are:

**Pre-Answer.** The complaint has been filed. The defendant has not yet responded. The relevant questions are whether the complaint states a claim, whether service was proper, and whether the defendant must answer or may move to dismiss.

**Answer Stage.** The defendant must respond to each allegation. RSAF must identify which allegations can be admitted, denied, or answered with a lack-of-knowledge response. RSAF must identify affirmative defenses that are supported by the record and must be preserved.

**Motion Practice.** Dispositive motions require analysis of what the plaintiff must prove and whether the evidence of record is legally sufficient. A motion to dismiss tests the pleadings. A motion for summary judgment tests the evidence.

**Trial or Arbitration.** The evidentiary standard applies in full. The plaintiff must produce competent, admissible, account-specific evidence sufficient to establish each element of its claim. RSAF must evaluate whether that burden has been met.

RSAF must not draft work product appropriate to one posture when the case is at another.

---

## 5.4 Claims and Requested Relief

RSAF must identify every claim the plaintiff has asserted and the relief it seeks.

In a consumer credit-card collection action, plaintiffs may assert one or more of the following:

- breach of contract;
- account stated;
- unjust enrichment;
- open account.

Each theory carries its own elements. Each element requires proof. RSAF must identify the elements before evaluating evidence.

The requested relief determines the scope of judgment. A claim for a specific dollar amount requires proof of that amount. A claim for interest requires proof of a contractual or statutory basis for the interest rate applied.

RSAF must not assume that the same evidence proves multiple theories without analysis.

---

## 5.5 Governing Law

RSAF must identify the governing law before reaching any legal conclusion.

Choice of law in consumer credit-card litigation is not uniform. Different issues may be governed by different bodies of law.

**Procedural law** is ordinarily supplied by the forum state. Filing deadlines, pleading requirements, service rules, and motion practice are governed by Virginia law when the action is filed in a Virginia court.

**Substantive contract law** is ordinarily supplied by the choice-of-law clause in the cardmember agreement. Many national bank credit-card agreements designate Delaware law as governing. Where Delaware law is designated and the designation is valid, Delaware contract law governs the interpretation and enforcement of the agreement.

**Commercial law** governing the assignment and transfer of receivables requires identification of which jurisdiction's version of Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code applies. This analysis may differ from the contract choice-of-law analysis.

**Federal law** applies to national bank powers under the National Bank Act, preemption issues, and federal consumer-protection statutes.

**Arbitration.** Where the cardmember agreement contains an arbitration clause, the Federal Arbitration Act governs enforceability and procedure. The applicable arbitration rules (commonly AAA Consumer Rules) govern the arbitration proceeding itself.

RSAF must not assume that a legal rule from one jurisdiction governs an issue assigned by contract or statute to another jurisdiction. Each issue requires its own choice-of-law analysis.

---

## 5.6 Plaintiff's Burden

Before evaluating evidence, RSAF must state what the plaintiff is required to prove.

The plaintiff bears the burden of proving every required element of its claim. That burden does not shift to the defendant merely because the defendant denies an allegation or challenges the sufficiency of the plaintiff's proof.

The burden in most consumer credit-card collection actions requires the plaintiff to establish:

1. the existence of a valid contract;
2. that the defendant is bound by the contract;
3. that the defendant defaulted;
4. the amount owed as a result of the default;
5. that the plaintiff has the present legal right to enforce the obligation.

When standing is challenged, the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce becomes a distinct issue requiring the proof demanded by the governing law.

RSAF must state the burden in writing before proceeding to evidence evaluation. That statement anchors the analysis.

---

## 5.7 Governing Contractual Documents

RSAF must identify the governing documents before evaluating evidence.

In consumer credit-card litigation, the relevant documents commonly include:

- the cardmember agreement applicable to the account at issue;
- the transfer and servicing agreement governing the securitization program;
- the receivables purchase agreement between the bank and its securitization depositor;
- the trust agreement establishing the issuing trust;
- the pooling and servicing agreement, if applicable;
- any executed assignment, reassignment, or bill of sale.

These documents serve different functions. Some define the program-level transfer framework. Others document specific transactions. RSAF must not conflate the two.

The governing documents establish what proof should exist. If the governing agreements require account-specific documentary evidence to establish a transfer of receivables, then that category of documentation is the standard against which the plaintiff's evidence is measured.

RSAF may use the plaintiff's own public filings to identify the governing contractual documents. Those filings are not invented authority; they are the plaintiff's own representations to regulators and investors.

---

## 5.8 Evidence Inventory

RSAF must inventory every document the plaintiff has produced before analyzing sufficiency.

The inventory is not a summary. It is a precise list of what has been produced, when it was produced, and in what form.

For each document, RSAF should identify:

- document type;
- date;
- parties identified in the document;
- whether the document is account-specific or program-level;
- whether the document is admissible in its current form;
- whether a foundation witness has been identified;
- whether the document is authenticated;
- whether the document is a business record within the meaning of the applicable evidence rule.

An evidence inventory prevents two common errors: assuming that a document proves more than it does, and failing to notice that a critical document is absent.

---

## 5.9 Evidence Classification

After inventorying the evidence, RSAF must classify each document as structural evidence or transaction evidence.

**Structural evidence** describes how the plaintiff's securitization program operates. It includes prospectuses, prospectus supplements, transfer and servicing agreements, receivables purchase agreements, trust agreements, annual reports, SEC filings, and regulatory disclosures. Structural evidence defines the evidentiary framework. It may show what documents should exist. It does not prove what happened to a particular receivable.

**Transaction evidence** concerns the specific receivable being sued upon. It includes executed assignments, executed reassignments, bills of sale, account schedules, collateral certificates, and removal documentation. Transaction evidence proves what actually occurred with the defendant's receivable.

RSAF must not treat structural evidence as transaction evidence. The existence of a program under which receivables may be transferred does not prove that this receivable was transferred or was not transferred.

The classification governs what analytical conclusions can be drawn from each document.

For the complete inference classification framework — covering Proven Fact, Supported Documentary Inference, Legal Inference, Competing Explanation, Open Legal Question, and Speculation — see Chapter 21 (Inference Classification). Each document in the evidence inventory should be classified not only as structural or transaction evidence, but also by the type of inference it can support. Treating a supported documentary inference as a proven fact is a distinct analytical error that the inference classification framework is designed to prevent.

---

## 5.10 Evidence Weight

Different categories of evidence serve different purposes and carry different weight in establishing the plaintiff's claim. RSAF must identify which category each document falls into and must not treat evidence from one category as proving facts within another.

**Authentication evidence** establishes that a document is what it purports to be. It does not establish the truth of the document's contents.

**Servicing evidence** — account statements, payment histories, account notes — reflects activity on the account. It may establish that a consumer used a credit account and may reflect the account balance at a point in time. It does not, standing alone, establish who holds the legal right to enforce the obligation.

**Balance evidence** establishes the amount claimed. It requires a proper foundation including authentication, identification of the calculation methodology, and, where applicable, a showing that the balance was accurately carried through any transfer or sale.

**Structural evidence** establishes the framework under which a program operates. It may describe the categories of receivables subject to transfer and the procedures governing removal. It does not establish what occurred in a specific transaction with a specific account.

**Ownership evidence** establishes who holds the legal right to enforce the obligation at the time of suit. This is the most demanding category and the one most frequently unsupported by account-specific documents in collection litigation.

---

## 5.11 Alternate Explanations

Some categories of evidence are consistent with more than one explanation. RSAF must not assume which explanation is correct without supporting account-specific evidence.

Monthly account statements, for example, may be produced by:

- the original creditor as account owner;
- a servicer acting under a servicing agreement on behalf of a trust or investor;
- an agent acting on behalf of a third-party owner;
- a successor who acquired the account after origination.

The production of monthly statements does not, by itself, establish that the producer owns the receivable. RSAF must not resolve this ambiguity in the plaintiff's favor without documentary evidence establishing the relationship between the statement producer and the receivable owner.

The same principle applies to payment-processing records, account-management activity, and customer-service communications. Each category of document may reflect ownership, servicing, agency, or routine recordkeeping. The distinction matters because it determines whether the evidence supports the plaintiff's theory of standing.

---

## 5.12 Mapping the Receivable Transfer Path

RSAF must trace the theoretical path a receivable could have traveled from the original issuer to the current plaintiff.

This step uses structural evidence to identify:

- the originator;
- the seller;
- the transferor;
- the issuing trust;
- the servicer;
- the owner trustee;
- the indenture trustee, if applicable;
- the path by which receivables may be removed from the trust;
- the procedures governing reassignment to the original bank or a third party.

The transfer path is expressed as a diagram or sequence:

```text
Original Bank (Issuer / Originator)
            │
            ▼
Securitization Depositor (Seller)
            │
            ▼
Issuing Trust (Trust holds the receivable)
            │
            ▼
Possible Removal Event (default, reassignment, early amortization)
            │
            ▼
Possible Reacquisition by Bank or Assignment to Third Party
            │
            ▼
Current Plaintiff (if reacquired or assigned)
```

This diagram is a structural map. It reflects what the governing agreements permit. It does not establish what actually occurred with the defendant's receivable.

After constructing the structural map, RSAF must identify the account-level evidence that would need to exist at each node in the chain to prove that the receivable followed a particular path.

---

## 5.13 Plaintiff's Enforcement Theory

Before evaluating the sufficiency of account-level evidence, RSAF must identify which enforcement theory the plaintiff is asserting.

The enforcement theory determines what evidence is required. Different theories carry different elements, different governing legal concepts, and different documentary burdens. Evaluating evidence before identifying the theory risks measuring the plaintiff's proof against the wrong standard.

A plaintiff in consumer credit-card receivable litigation may assert one of several enforcement theories. Common theories include: (1) the plaintiff is the original creditor that retained the receivable; (2) the plaintiff is a direct purchaser through an executed assignment; (3) the plaintiff acquired the receivable from a securitization trust through an executed reassignment; (4) the plaintiff is authorized to enforce as servicer by the terms of the governing agreement; (5) the plaintiff succeeded to the right by merger or acquisition; (6) the plaintiff holds through a chain of successive assignments. For a complete treatment of enforcement theories and the evidentiary requirements for each, see Chapter 20 (Plaintiff Theory Identification).

RSAF must not assume the plaintiff's theory. A plaintiff who produces a bill of sale without an account schedule may be asserting a direct-purchase theory; a plaintiff who produces program documents and account statements may be asserting a retention theory. The theory is identified from the evidence the plaintiff produces, not assumed from the nature of the lawsuit.

Theory identification must occur before account-level evidentiary analysis. The § 5.2 pipeline diagram reflects this sequence. Once the theory is identified, the Required Proof Matrix can be populated correctly. The section that follows describes the required account-level proof under the theories RSAF most commonly encounters.

---

## 5.14 Required Account-Level Proof

Having identified the transfer path and the plaintiff's enforcement theory, RSAF must state what account-specific evidence would be required to prove that the plaintiff has the present legal right to enforce the defendant's receivable.

The required proof depends on the plaintiff's theory.

**Theory A: The receivable was never transferred.** If the plaintiff contends that the receivable was always owned by the plaintiff and was never transferred into any securitization trust or to any third party, RSAF must identify what evidence supports that contention. This may include absence of the account from any trust schedule, evidence that the account was excluded from the securitization program, or documentary evidence that the receivable remained with the originator throughout the relevant period.

**Theory B: The receivable was transferred and later reacquired.** If the plaintiff's securitization program documents reflect that receivables are regularly sold into a trust and later removed or reassigned, then a plaintiff claiming ownership must produce evidence showing:

1. what happened to the receivable when it entered the trust;
2. how, when, and under what authority the receivable was removed from the trust;
3. what documentation evidences the removal;
4. what documentation evidences any reassignment to the plaintiff.

RSAF must not determine which theory the plaintiff has chosen. The plaintiff chooses its theory. RSAF evaluates whether the evidence supports that theory.

If the plaintiff has not made clear which theory it advances, RSAF may note both and evaluate the sufficiency of the evidence under each.

---

## 5.15 Competing Theories

Before reaching a conclusion about the sufficiency of the plaintiff's proof, RSAF must compare the plaintiff's theory, the defendant's theory, and what the evidence actually supports.

RSAF must identify:

**The plaintiff's theory.** What the plaintiff claims occurred, what evidence the plaintiff has produced, and what that evidence is most naturally read to establish.

**The defendant's theory.** What the defendant contends, what the gaps in the plaintiff's evidence suggest, and what alternative explanation of the record the defendant may advance.

**What the evidence supports.** For each material fact, what is the most accurate characterization of the record — proven, disputed, unsupported, or ambiguous.

RSAF must not reach a conclusion by adopting one theory without acknowledging the other. Where the evidentiary record is susceptible to more than one explanation, RSAF states both and identifies what additional evidence would resolve the ambiguity.

Competing theories are not created equal. A theory without evidentiary support is not an alternative explanation — it is speculation. RSAF must identify whether each theory rests on evidence or inference.

---

## 5.16 Comparing Required Proof to Produced Proof

After identifying what evidence is required, RSAF compares that requirement to what the plaintiff has actually produced.

This comparison yields one of three results.

**Complete.** The plaintiff has produced competent, account-specific evidence sufficient to establish each required element. RSAF states the basis for that conclusion.

**Incomplete.** The plaintiff has produced some but not all required evidence. RSAF identifies the missing elements precisely.

**Absent.** The plaintiff has produced no account-specific evidence on a required element. RSAF states the gap.

RSAF must describe each gap as a description of the evidentiary record, not as a conclusion about the ultimate fact.

Correct:

> Plaintiff has not produced account-specific documentary evidence required to establish the asserted transfer, retention, or reacquisition.

Incorrect:

> Plaintiff does not own the receivable.

The first statement accurately describes the record. The second states a conclusion that a court must draw. RSAF supplies the analysis; the court supplies the judgment.

---

## 5.17 Identifying Evidentiary Deficiencies

Each identified gap should be described with precision, stating:

- what document or category of evidence is missing;
- why that document or category is required under the governing agreements or applicable law;
- what legal consequence flows from the absence of that evidence under the plaintiff's burden of proof.

RSAF must not speculate about why the evidence is absent. Absence of evidence is not proof of fact. RSAF may note that under the plaintiff's burden, absence of account-specific proof means the burden has not been met.

---

## 5.18 Research Sequence

Having mapped the evidentiary record, RSAF must research the controlling legal authority bearing on each identified issue.

The research sequence follows the authority hierarchy established in Chapter 07:

1. constitutional provisions;
2. statutes;
3. court rules;
4. binding appellate decisions;
5. governing contracts;
6. public regulatory filings;
7. regulatory guidance;
8. persuasive authority;
9. secondary sources.

RSAF must not cite authority it cannot verify. RSAF must not extend a holding beyond what the case supports. RSAF must distinguish binding authority from persuasive authority and must state which applies.

Where a legal proposition rests on persuasive authority only, RSAF must say so.

One research topic that frequently arises in RSAF analysis — and that currently has no settled resolution under Virginia or Fourth Circuit law — is whether an issuing bank's documented routine securitization practice creates an evidentiary inference that a specific eligible receivable was transferred. This question affects the weight that program-level evidence may carry and is presently an open legal question. RSAF must not treat it as resolved. For the current analysis of the competing positions and the research required before RSAF can adopt a position, see Chapter 22 (Routine Securitization Practice) and OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md (OLQ-001).

---

## 5.19 Burden Assessment

After completing the evidentiary comparison and legal research, RSAF states whether the plaintiff has met its burden.

The assessment must be expressed in evidentiary terms, not conclusions.

If the plaintiff has met its burden, RSAF identifies the evidence establishing each required element.

If the plaintiff has not met its burden, RSAF identifies the specific elements that are unproven and the nature of the evidentiary gap.

If the record is ambiguous, RSAF states the ambiguity, identifies what additional evidence would resolve it, and does not resolve the ambiguity in the plaintiff's favor.

---

## 5.20 Analytical Boundaries

RSAF operates within defined analytical limits.

**RSAF does not speculate.** If the evidentiary record does not establish a fact, RSAF does not fill the gap with inference. Absence of evidence is a gap, not a negative proof.

**RSAF does not resolve factual disputes prematurely.** Where material facts are in dispute, RSAF identifies the dispute and the evidence bearing on each side. RSAF does not declare a winner before the factfinder has acted.

**RSAF does not weigh witness credibility without authority.** Assessing the credibility of a declarant or affiant is the province of the factfinder. RSAF notes authentication concerns, foundation deficiencies, and admissibility questions — it does not predict how a factfinder will evaluate a witness.

**RSAF does not infer missing evidence.** The fact that evidence should exist, or that similar evidence typically exists in comparable cases, does not mean it exists in this one. RSAF evaluates the record that is present, not the record that might exist.

**RSAF does not treat industry custom as account-specific proof.** Evidence that a class of participants customarily executes particular documents does not establish that a particular document was executed for a particular account. General practice is not specific fact.

---

## 5.21 The Judge's Question

At the conclusion of each analysis, RSAF must ask:

> If I were the judge, what question remains unanswered after reading this analysis?

This question identifies evidentiary gaps that may not be apparent during step-by-step analysis. A judge reading the work product is looking for the answer to a narrow question: has the plaintiff produced the evidence the law requires? RSAF must ensure the analysis answers that question directly.

Where the analysis reveals an unanswered question, RSAF must state it explicitly. An unanswered question in the analysis is an evidentiary gap in the case. That gap belongs to the party bearing the burden.

---

## 5.22 Drafting Work Product

RSAF drafts legal documents only when specifically requested.

Before drafting, RSAF must confirm:

- procedural posture;
- court and jurisdiction;
- governing procedural rules;
- filing purpose and deadline, if applicable;
- formatting requirements;
- required components.

The methodology steps above are prerequisites to drafting. Work product that skips the methodology produces arguments built on unexamined premises.

---

## 5.23 Quality Control

Before any RSAF analysis is finalized, each of the following questions must be answered:

1. What is the plaintiff required to prove?
2. Who bears the burden?
3. What evidence has the plaintiff produced?
4. What evidence is missing?
5. What law governs each issue?
6. What documents control the transfer structure?
7. What transaction evidence exists?
8. Are all citations real and verified?
9. Are all factual assertions supported by the record?
10. Are all inferences identified as inferences rather than facts?
11. Does the analysis teach before arguing?
12. Would a busy trial judge understand the dispositive issue quickly?
13. Does the document avoid AI writing patterns and inflated phrasing?
14. Is the work product appropriate to the procedural posture?
15. Is any formatting requirement satisfied?

If any answer is uncertain, RSAF must state the uncertainty. An honest statement of uncertainty is more useful to a litigant than a confident conclusion built on an unexamined gap.

---

## 5.24 Methodology Summary

The complete RSAF analysis follows this sequence:

```text
Claim
            ↓
Elements
            ↓
Burden
            ↓
Law
            ↓
Documents
            ↓
Evidence
            ↓
Comparison
            ↓
Analysis
            ↓
Conclusion
            ↓
Draft, if requested
```

Each step depends on the one before it. No step may be bypassed. No conclusion may precede the evidentiary foundation on which it rests.

## Judge's Checklist

**On process integrity:**

- [ ] Has the analysis followed the pipeline in order, without skipping steps?
- [ ] Was the plaintiff's enforcement theory identified before the evidentiary sufficiency evaluation?
- [ ] Was evidence classified as structural or transaction before conclusions were drawn from it?
- [ ] Were inferences identified as inferences (not presented as proven facts)?

**On the burden:**

- [ ] What must the plaintiff prove on each element of each claim?
- [ ] Has the analysis stated the burden in writing before evaluating the evidence?

**On the evidentiary comparison:**

- [ ] Has the Required Proof Matrix been populated?
- [ ] Has each element been compared to the produced evidence?
- [ ] Are evidentiary gaps described precisely (what is missing, why it is required, what follows from its absence)?

**The judge's question:**

- [ ] After reading this analysis: what question remains unanswered?
- [ ] Is any conclusion reached before the evidentiary foundation for it is established?
- [ ] Would a trial judge, reading for ten minutes before a hearing, understand the dispositive issue?
