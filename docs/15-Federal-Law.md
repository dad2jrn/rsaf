# 15 — Federal Law

## 15.1 Scope and Research Rule

This chapter identifies the federal legal frameworks most frequently relevant to consumer credit-card receivable litigation. Federal law intersects with this subject in several distinct ways: it governs the organizational structure of national banks, preempts certain state law claims, protects consumers against abusive collection practices, and regulates the arbitration of consumer disputes.

**Research Rule.** This chapter identifies applicable frameworks and key statutes. Before citing any federal statute, regulation, or case in a court-facing document, verify the current text of the statute, the applicable regulatory provisions, and the precise judicial holding. This chapter is a research guide, not a primary source.

---

## 15.2 The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act

**Statute.** 15 U.S.C. § 1692 et seq.

The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) prohibits abusive, deceptive, and unfair debt collection practices by "debt collectors."

**Scope.** The FDCPA's coverage depends on whether the plaintiff qualifies as a "debt collector" as the statute defines that term. The statute generally defines a debt collector as a person who regularly collects debts owed to another. Creditors collecting their own debts — that is, debts the creditor originated and has continuously owned — are generally excluded from the definition. Debt buyers who acquire defaulted debt and collect it in their own name are generally **not** "debt collectors" under the FDCPA. The Supreme Court unanimously resolved the core question in *Henson v. Santander Consumer USA, Inc.*, 582 U.S. 79 (2017): the statutory phrase "debts owed or due another" does not cover an entity that owns the debt it collects. Whether a particular debt-buyer plaintiff qualifies as a debt collector therefore turns on whether it acts as a purchaser-owner or as a servicer collecting on behalf of a third party.

**Henson v. Santander Consumer USA, Inc., 582 U.S. 79 (2017).** The Supreme Court unanimously held that an entity that purchases defaulted debt for its own account and then seeks to collect it is not a "debt collector" under the FDCPA. The Court's analysis turned on the statutory text: the FDCPA defines a debt collector as one who regularly collects debts owed or due "another." A purchaser who owns the debt collects money owed to itself, not to another, and therefore falls outside the statutory definition. This holding is binding on all federal courts.

*Ownership structure controls.* FDCPA coverage depends on how the collecting entity holds the debt at the time of collection:

- A servicer that collects on behalf of a debt owner who is a third party may qualify as a debt collector.
- A purchaser that has acquired ownership of the debt and collects for its own account generally does not qualify.

*Practitioner note — verify before court use.* Before asserting or opposing FDCPA coverage as to any debt-buyer plaintiff, verify: (a) the precise holding of *Henson v. Santander Consumer USA, Inc.*, 582 U.S. 79 (2017), (b) any subsequent circuit-level decisions applying or distinguishing *Henson* in the forum jurisdiction, and (c) whether the plaintiff actually purchased the debt or acted in a servicer capacity — that fact-specific question remains subject to evidentiary proof. Do not cite this case in any court-facing document without independently confirming the citation and the current state of the law in the forum.

**RSAF analysis.** Before asserting or evaluating any FDCPA obligation, RSAF must classify the plaintiff:

1. Is the plaintiff the original creditor?
2. Has the plaintiff continuously held the debt, or did it acquire the debt after default?
3. Does the plaintiff collect debts on behalf of others, or only its own debts?

If the plaintiff is not a debt collector under the FDCPA's definition, the FDCPA does not apply to its collection activities. RSAF must not assert FDCPA obligations against a plaintiff that the statute does not cover.

**Relevant FDCPA provisions.**

- 15 U.S.C. § 1692e: Prohibits false, deceptive, or misleading representations, including misrepresenting the character, amount, or legal status of a debt or the identity of the current creditor.
- 15 U.S.C. § 1692g: Requires a debt collector to provide validation information and to cease collection during verification periods upon written request by the consumer.
- 15 U.S.C. § 1692k: Provides for civil liability, including actual damages, statutory damages, and attorney fees for violations.

**Verify:** Confirm the current text of the applicable FDCPA provisions and any circuit court interpretations applicable in the relevant jurisdiction.

## 15.3 The Federal Arbitration Act

**Statute.** 9 U.S.C. § 1 et seq.

The Federal Arbitration Act (FAA) governs the enforceability of written arbitration agreements in contracts involving commerce.

**Enforceability.** 9 U.S.C. § 2 provides that a written provision in a contract evidencing a transaction in commerce to settle disputes by arbitration shall be valid, irrevocable, and enforceable, except upon grounds that exist in law or equity for the revocation of any contract. This provision preempts state laws that single out arbitration agreements for disfavored treatment.

**Scope in consumer credit-card cases.** Most national bank credit-card agreements include arbitration clauses. Where the clause is valid and covers the dispute, the defendant may move to compel arbitration. The court must grant the motion if the arbitration clause is enforceable and covers the dispute at issue.

**Class-action waivers.** The Supreme Court has held that the FAA preempts state law rules that categorically prohibit enforcement of class-action waivers in arbitration agreements. See AT&T Mobility LLC v. Concepcion, 563 U.S. 333 (2011). Class-action waivers in credit-card arbitration agreements are therefore generally enforceable under federal law.

**Arbitration procedure.** Where arbitration is compelled, the applicable arbitration rules — commonly the American Arbitration Association Consumer Arbitration Rules — govern the proceeding. RSAF's evidence and burden analysis applies in arbitration as in court, though specific procedural rules may differ.

## 15.4 The National Bank Act and Federal Preemption

**Statute.** 12 U.S.C. § 1 et seq.

National banks are chartered under the National Bank Act and regulated primarily by the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency (OCC). The OCC's regulatory framework and the National Bank Act may preempt certain state consumer-protection laws as applied to national banks.

**Relevant preemption issues.** The scope of National Bank Act preemption is a matter of ongoing litigation and regulatory interpretation. RSAF identifies the following as potential preemption issues:

- State law limitations on interest rates charged by national banks (generally preempted for national banks under Marquette Nat'l Bank v. First of Omaha Service Corp., 439 U.S. 299 (1978), and subsequent decisions).
- State licensing requirements for debt collection by national banks.
- State law requirements that may conflict with OCC regulations governing national bank operations.

**Caution.** Preemption analysis is fact-specific and jurisdiction-specific. RSAF must identify the specific state law claim, the specific federal provision or regulation asserted to preempt it, and the applicable circuit court authority before concluding that preemption applies.

## 15.5 SEC Regulation and Public Filings

National bank credit-card securitization programs are subject to SEC disclosure requirements. The SEC's Regulation AB (17 C.F.R. Part 229) governs disclosure requirements for asset-backed securities offerings, including credit-card receivables programs.

**Relevance to RSAF.** The SEC filings made by the issuing trust and the originating bank — prospectuses, prospectus supplements, annual reports on Form 10-K, monthly distribution reports, and similar filings — are publicly available and constitute the plaintiff's own representations to regulators and investors.

RSAF uses these filings as structural evidence: they describe the program, identify the participants, describe the transfer mechanics, and specify the required documentation. They are not account-specific evidence of any transfer, but they define the evidentiary framework against which the plaintiff's account-specific proof is measured.

**Limits.** SEC filings are prepared for investor disclosure purposes. They do not independently prove what occurred with any specific receivable.

## 15.6 FDIC Guidance on True-Sale Treatment

The FDIC has issued guidance addressing the circumstances under which it will respect the legal separateness of asset transfers in a bank receivership — that is, when it will treat receivables as having been effectively sold rather than as remaining assets of the bank.

RSAF identifies FDIC regulatory guidance as a potential source of authority on:

- the true-sale characterization of transfers to bankruptcy-remote entities;
- the conditions the FDIC requires before recognizing a securitization transfer as effective in a bank receivership;
- the interplay between FDIC treatment and the "safe harbor" for qualifying asset transfers.

**Caution.** FDIC guidance is regulatory in nature. It does not bind courts in civil collection litigation. RSAF must distinguish between the FDIC's regulatory standards and the legal standards applicable in civil proceedings. The two may differ. Do not cite FDIC guidance as binding legal authority without identifying it accurately as regulatory guidance.

**Citation.** The FDIC Safe Harbor Rule is codified at 12 C.F.R. § 360.6. The current version was finalized in March 2020 (effective May 4, 2020) and amended by technical correction in April 2021. Any citation must identify the version of the rule in effect at the time of the relevant transfer, as earlier versions imposed different conditions.

## 15.7 Federal Consumer Financial Protection

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) has issued rules and guidance on debt collection, credit reporting, and fair lending relevant to the credit-card context.

RSAF identifies the following as potential CFPB-related research areas:

- CFPB rules implementing the FDCPA (Regulation F, 12 C.F.R. Part 1006);
- CFPB guidance on what constitutes adequate validation of a debt for purposes of the FDCPA;
- CFPB supervisory expectations for debt buyers and servicers of consumer receivables.

CFPB rules and guidance are relevant where the plaintiff is a debt collector subject to FDCPA jurisdiction. They do not generally apply to original creditors collecting their own debts.

## Judge's Checklist

**On the FDCPA:**

- [ ] Is the plaintiff a "debt collector" as the FDCPA defines that term?
- [ ] Original creditors collecting their own continuously held debts are generally not "debt collectors." Has the plaintiff's classification been established?
- [ ] If the plaintiff is a debt buyer or third-party collector: has it complied with debt validation requirements?

**On the FAA and arbitration:**

- [ ] Does the cardmember agreement contain a binding arbitration clause?
- [ ] Has the defendant moved to compel arbitration?
- [ ] If arbitration is appropriate: has the defense been raised before significant litigation conduct that might constitute waiver?

**On preemption:**

- [ ] Is the plaintiff a national bank or operating subsidiary?
- [ ] If so: certain state law claims regarding interest rates and credit terms may be preempted by the National Bank Act. Identify which claims, if any, are subject to preemption before raising them.

**On regulatory guidance:**

- [ ] FDIC Safe Harbor Rule and CFPB guidance are not binding on courts in civil collection litigation. Has any regulatory guidance been cited at the correct level of authority (persuasive, not controlling)?
