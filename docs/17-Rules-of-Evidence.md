# 17 — Rules of Evidence

## 17.1 Purpose

RSAF's evidentiary analysis is only as useful as its understanding of what evidence is admissible and what foundation is required to admit it. This chapter explains the evidentiary principles most relevant to consumer credit-card collection litigation, with specific attention to business records, authentication, hearsay, and the documentary evidence on which standing arguments depend.

**Research Rule.** The specific rules governing admissibility vary by forum. Virginia courts apply the Virginia Rules of Evidence. Arbitration proceedings apply the applicable arbitration administrator's rules, which often afford more flexibility. This chapter describes general evidentiary principles. Before applying any specific rule in a court-facing document, verify the applicable rule in the forum court.

---

## 17.2 The Threshold: Admissibility

Before a piece of evidence may be considered by a court, it must clear the admissibility threshold. Admissibility is a preliminary question decided by the court. Evidence that is not admissible may not be relied upon regardless of its apparent content.

The primary admissibility requirements relevant to RSAF are:

- **Relevance.** The evidence must tend to make a fact of consequence more or less probable.
- **Authentication.** The evidence must be authenticated as what it purports to be.
- **Hearsay.** A statement offered for the truth of the matter asserted must satisfy a recognized exception to the hearsay rule or fall outside the rule's scope.
- **Original document rules.** Where the content of a writing is at issue, the original or a permissible substitute must generally be produced.

Each document in the evidentiary record must clear each applicable threshold. RSAF must evaluate admissibility before evaluating the probative weight of any document.

---

## 17.3 Authentication

Authentication is establishing that a document is what it purports to be. An unauthenticated document is not admissible regardless of what it says.

Authentication may be accomplished through:

**Testimony of a witness with personal knowledge.** A witness who has personal knowledge of the document's creation, contents, or custodial history may testify to its authenticity.

**Business records certification.** Many jurisdictions permit authentication through a written declaration from a qualified custodian that satisfies the foundational requirements for the business records exception. The declaration must address each element of the foundation.

**Distinctive characteristics.** A document may be authenticated through its distinctive characteristics — its appearance, content, internal patterns — in combination with circumstances.

**Stipulation or admission.** Parties may stipulate to authenticity, relieving the proponent of the authentication burden.

**What authentication does not establish.** Authentication establishes that the document is genuine. It does not establish the truth of the document's contents. A properly authenticated account statement is still subject to hearsay analysis if offered for the truth of a matter recorded in it.

---

## 17.4 The Business Records Exception

The business records exception to the hearsay rule is the primary vehicle through which account statements, payment histories, and similar records are admitted in consumer receivable litigation.

**Foundation elements.** The business records exception typically requires proof that:

1. the record was made at or near the time of the event recorded;
2. the record was made by — or from information transmitted by — a person with knowledge of the act, event, or condition recorded;
3. the record was kept in the course of a regularly conducted business activity;
4. creating records of this type was a regular practice of that business activity;
5. there is no indication that the source of information or the method or circumstances of preparation lacks trustworthiness.

**Who may lay the foundation.** The foundation may be established by the custodian of records or another qualified witness — not necessarily the person who created the record, but someone with knowledge of the system under which it was created and maintained. Where the foundation is provided through affidavit or declaration rather than live testimony, the affidavit must satisfy each foundational element.

**The untrustworthiness exception.** Even where the technical foundation is satisfied, a court may exclude a business record if the circumstances suggest the record is untrustworthy. Records prepared in anticipation of litigation may face this challenge.

---

## 17.5 Acquired Records and Foundation Complications

A recurring challenge in consumer receivable litigation arises when a plaintiff relies on records that originated with another entity — for example, a debt buyer that acquired account records with a portfolio purchase.

**The integration question.** A custodian employed by the current plaintiff can lay the business records foundation for those records only if the plaintiff has integrated them into its own regularly conducted business activities and the custodian has knowledge of the system under which the records were created and maintained. A custodian who merely received and stored records from the original creditor without incorporating them into a distinct business process may not satisfy the foundation.

**The originating entity's practices.** To lay a full foundation for an acquired record, the proponent may need to establish not only how the current holder maintains the records but also how the originating entity created and maintained them.

**Practical implication for RSAF.** When a plaintiff's authentication evidence is provided by a records custodian who was not employed by the originating creditor, RSAF must evaluate whether the custodian's affidavit addresses the foundational requirements for the records of the originating entity. An affidavit that merely says "I am a custodian of records and I have reviewed the account" may be insufficient without more.

---

## 17.6 Hearsay and Its Exceptions

Hearsay is an out-of-court statement offered to prove the truth of the matter asserted. Most of the documentary evidence in consumer receivable litigation — account statements, payment records, account notes — is hearsay. It is admitted only through an applicable exception.

**Business records exception.** See Section 17.4. This is the primary exception used in receivable litigation.

**Records of regularly conducted activity.** Some jurisdictions use this terminology, substantially equivalent to the business records exception. The foundational elements are similar.

**Party admissions.** Statements made by a party to the litigation may be admissible against that party as admissions. If a plaintiff has made representations in SEC filings, annual reports, or prospectuses, those representations may be offered against the plaintiff without satisfying the business records exception.

**What is not covered.** The business records exception covers records generated by the business in the regular course of its activities. It does not automatically cover statements embedded in those records that were made by outsiders. An account record that includes a note from a consumer or a third-party communication may contain hearsay within hearsay — each layer requires its own admissibility analysis.

---

## 17.7 Affidavit vs. Live Testimony

In motion practice and some arbitration proceedings, evidence is presented through affidavit rather than live testimony. RSAF must understand the implications of this distinction.

**Affidavit evidence.** An affidavit is a written statement sworn under penalty of perjury. It may be used in support of or in opposition to a motion. For summary judgment, each party may submit affidavits to establish or dispute material facts.

**Foundation through affidavit.** Where applicable court rules permit, the business records foundation may be established through a written certification or declaration rather than live testimony. The certification must satisfy the same foundational requirements as live testimony.

**Cross-examination.** Affidavit testimony is not subject to cross-examination at the time it is submitted. Where credibility or the completeness of the foundation is genuinely in dispute, the absence of cross-examination opportunity is significant. A court may decline to admit records where the foundational affidavit is conclusory or where the affiant's knowledge is disputed.

**What a custodian affidavit does and does not prove.** A custodian affidavit properly laying the business records foundation authenticates the records it describes. It does not thereby prove every factual assertion contained in those records, nor does it establish chain of title unless the records themselves, when authenticated, document the transfer path.

---

## 17.8 Electronic Records

Account records in consumer credit-card litigation are typically electronic. Electronic records present specific authentication questions.

**System integrity.** Authentication of electronic records may require a showing that the system that generated them is reliable and that the records were accurately stored and retrieved without alteration.

**Metadata and audit trails.** Some courts have considered metadata, audit trails, and similar electronic evidence when assessing the authenticity and trustworthiness of electronic business records.

**Electronic versus paper records.** Original document rules in most jurisdictions have been adapted to address electronically stored information. A printed version of an electronic record is generally an admissible duplicate where there is no genuine question about the accuracy of the printout.

---

## 17.9 Admissibility in Arbitration

In arbitration proceedings under the AAA Consumer Arbitration Rules or similar frameworks, the formal rules of evidence do not apply in the same way as in court. Arbitrators generally have broad discretion to admit evidence and assign it appropriate weight.

This does not mean that foundational deficiencies are irrelevant in arbitration. RSAF must still identify:

- whether documents have been properly identified;
- whether the proponent has provided a sufficient foundation to establish trustworthiness;
- what weight the evidence should carry on each element.

In arbitration, a foundational deficiency may go to weight rather than admissibility. RSAF should identify this distinction when analyzing evidence in an arbitration context.

## 17.10 Summary: RSAF's Evidentiary Checklist

For each document in the evidentiary record, RSAF must determine:

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Is the document relevant to an element of the claim? | |
| Has it been authenticated? By whom? By what method? | |
| Does it constitute hearsay when offered for the purpose the plaintiff intends? | |
| If hearsay, what exception applies? Are the foundational requirements met? | |
| If a business record, does the foundation address: time of creation, knowledge of the creator, regular course of business, regular practice of the business, no untrustworthiness? | |
| If acquired from another entity, does the foundation address the originating entity's records practices? | |
| Is the document an original or admissible duplicate? | |
| Are there hearsay-within-hearsay issues? | |
| Does the document prove what the plaintiff claims it proves, or does it prove something else? | |

This checklist is applied sequentially. A document that fails the authentication test does not reach the hearsay analysis. A document that clears authentication and hearsay but is account-level irrelevant — because it proves program structure but not account-specific events — contributes nothing to the standing element.

---

## Judge's Checklist

**Before admitting any document offered by plaintiff:**

- [ ] Has the document been authenticated? By live testimony or permissible certification?
- [ ] If authentication is by affidavit: does the affidavit address each foundational element, or is it conclusory ("I am a custodian and I have reviewed these records")?
- [ ] Is the document hearsay? If so, what exception applies?
- [ ] If business records exception: is the foundation complete (time of creation, knowledge of creator, regular course, regular practice, no untrustworthiness indicator)?

**On acquired records:**

- [ ] Were these records created by a different entity than the plaintiff?
- [ ] Does the custodian's foundation address the originating entity's recordkeeping — not just how the plaintiff stores the records today?

**The key distinction:**

- [ ] Does the document prove what the plaintiff says it proves, or something different?
- [ ] Account statements prove account activity — balance, charges, payments. They do not prove who currently holds the legal right to collect.
- [ ] An affidavit that asserts the plaintiff "owns" the account does not substitute for transfer documents that prove it.

**In arbitration:**

- [ ] Arbitrators have broader evidentiary discretion. Foundational deficiencies may go to weight rather than admissibility.
- [ ] RSAF should still identify the deficiency and argue that it reduces the document's probative value.
