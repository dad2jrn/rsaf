# 08 — Program-Level vs. Account-Level Evidence

## 8.1 Purpose

One of RSAF's defining analytical distinctions is the difference between program-level evidence and account-level evidence. These two categories serve different evidentiary functions. Confusing them is one of the most common errors in consumer receivable litigation.

This chapter explains what each category is, what each can and cannot prove, how RSAF uses program-level evidence to define the evidentiary framework, and where the limits of that use lie.

---

## 8.2 The Core Distinction

**Program-level evidence** describes how a bank's receivables program is structured and how it operates. It may show that a bank has established a program under which eligible credit-card receivables are periodically sold, assigned, transferred, and potentially removed and reassigned. Program-level evidence defines the framework.

**Account-level evidence** concerns what actually occurred with the specific receivable being sued upon. It documents the transfer, retention, removal, or reacquisition of one particular account. Account-level evidence proves what happened to this receivable.

The gap between these two categories is the gap between knowing that a transfer mechanism exists and knowing that this transfer mechanism was invoked for this account.

A plaintiff cannot satisfy its burden on the standing element by producing only program-level evidence. The existence of a transfer program does not establish that any particular receivable was transferred, retained, or reacquired under it.

---

## 8.3 Program-Level Evidence

Program-level evidence reflects the operational structure of the plaintiff's receivables program. It is typically found in publicly filed documents.

Common examples include:

- SEC prospectuses describing the issuance of asset-backed securities;
- Prospectus supplements filed with respect to specific series of securities;
- Receivables Purchase Agreements (RPAs) between the originating bank and a transferor entity;
- Transfer and Servicing Agreements (TSAs) between the transferor entity and an issuing trust;
- Pooling and Servicing Agreements (PSAs) in certain securitization structures;
- Trust agreements establishing the issuing entity;
- Indentures governing the notes issued by a trust;
- Annual reports filed with the SEC on Form 10-K, filed by either the corporate bank or the issuing trust;
- UCC financing statements covering pools of receivables transferred to a trust or transferor.

**What program-level evidence may establish.**

Program-level evidence establishes the plaintiff's own transfer framework — the structure the plaintiff itself created, publicly disclosed, and represented to regulators, investors, and counterparties. It is the plaintiff's own description of how its receivables move and how they are documented.

Specifically, program-level evidence may show:

- that the bank established a program under which eligible receivables are sold to a transferor entity;
- that the transferor entity transfers those receivables to an issuing trust;
- the identities of the parties: originator, transferor, trust, servicer, trustee;
- the contractual requirements for transfer — what documents must be executed, what schedules must be prepared, what procedures must be followed;
- the contractual procedures for removing receivables from the trust upon default, charge-off, or other specified events;
- the contractual procedures for reassigning removed receivables back to the transferor or the originating bank;
- what documentation the plaintiff's own framework requires at each step — so that the absence of such documentation is measured against the plaintiff's own stated requirements, not against an external standard imposed by the defense.

This last point is analytically important. When the plaintiff's own governing agreements require an executed account schedule to document a transfer, and the plaintiff has not produced one, the plaintiff has not met the standard it established for itself. RSAF uses program-level evidence to hold the plaintiff to its own documented framework.

**What program-level evidence cannot establish.**

Program-level evidence cannot establish:

- that the defendant's specific receivable was ever sold into the program;
- that the defendant's specific receivable remained in the trust at any particular time;
- that the defendant's specific receivable was removed under any particular procedure;
- that the defendant's specific receivable was reassigned to the current plaintiff;
- the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce the defendant's specific receivable.

---

## 8.4 Account-Level Evidence

Account-level evidence connects the specific defendant's receivable to the legal event being asserted: transfer, retention, removal, or reacquisition.

Common examples include:

- executed assignments identifying the defendant's account by account number, or referencing a schedule that identifies it;
- executed reassignments returning a specifically identified receivable from the trust or transferor to the plaintiff;
- account schedules attached to or incorporated into an executed transfer instrument, listing the defendant's account number;
- collateral certificates that identify a pool of receivables included in a trust interest and that, when combined with a schedule, identify the defendant's account;
- removal notices identifying the defendant's account as removed from the trust under governing agreement procedures;
- authenticated internal records specifically linking the defendant's receivable to the plaintiff through a documented chain.

**The specificity requirement.** Account-level evidence is distinguished by its connection to the specific receivable at issue. A document that identifies a broad pool of receivables without identifying the defendant's account may have limited account-level probative value unless the defendant's account can be established as part of that pool through additional evidence.

---

## 8.5 The RSAF Rule

Program-level documents define what proof should exist. Account-level documents prove whether that proof exists for this receivable.

Both are required for a complete analysis. Program-level evidence allows RSAF to identify the transfer path, the required documentation at each step, and the governing procedures. Account-level evidence then allows RSAF to determine whether those procedures were followed for this specific receivable and whether the required documents have been produced.

Neither category can substitute for the other.

---

## 8.6 Using Program Documents as the Plaintiff's Own Transfer Framework

RSAF uses program-level evidence as a framework tool — and specifically, as the plaintiff's own transfer framework.

The plaintiff's public documents are not invented by the defense. They are the plaintiff's own representations: to the SEC, to investors, to rating agencies, and to regulators. When the plaintiff argues that it owns a specific receivable, those public documents describe the path by which that ownership must have been established (or not disturbed). If the documents describe a transfer path, the plaintiff must show that this receivable followed that path. If they describe removal and reassignment procedures, the plaintiff must show that those procedures produced the documents they require.

When a bank's public documents establish that:

1. eligible receivables are sold from the bank to a bankruptcy-remote transferor;
2. the transferor transfers those receivables to an issuing trust;
3. transfers are evidenced by executed assignments and account schedules;
4. removed receivables are returned through a specific reassignment procedure and must be identified on a schedule;

then RSAF can identify what account-specific evidence should exist at each step. If the plaintiff asserts a present legal right to enforce a receivable that passed through that structure, the required account-level evidence includes documentation corresponding to each step in the plaintiff's own framework.

This is the proper use of program documents: not as proof that a transfer occurred, but as identification of what proof the plaintiff's own framework requires. The plaintiff then must produce that proof.

The plaintiff's own framework extends beyond contract. For banks that securitized receivables, the FDIC's Securitization Safe Harbor Rule (12 C.F.R. § 360.6) required the sponsoring bank to separately identify in its financial asset databases the financial assets transferred and to maintain closing documents for each securitization. A bank that operated a compliant securitization program therefore should have account-level records identifying what was transferred. The regulatory framework, like the contractual framework, required the creation of the very records now absent in litigation.

**The formulation RSAF uses:**

> Plaintiff's own public documents establish a securitization program under which eligible receivables are transferred through a defined chain, with each transfer evidenced by specific documentation. If the defendant's receivable was part of that program, the plaintiff should be able to produce account-specific evidence showing what happened to that receivable at each stage. Plaintiff has [produced / not produced] such evidence.

Program-level evidence does not establish the historical treatment of the defendant's specific receivable. A plaintiff who produces program documents but no account-specific transfer documentation has established the framework through which the defendant's receivable may have passed — not that it did pass through it, and not that the plaintiff has any present legal right to enforce it.

---

## 8.7 The Inference Principle and Its Limits

Where a bank's public documents show a recurring, comprehensive program under which eligible receivables are routinely transferred, RSAF may use those documents to identify the inference that would flow if no account-level evidence shows retention.

The inference is that a receivable from an account that meets the program's eligibility criteria may have been transferred under the program. This is an inference — not a proof. It may be relevant to identify what additional evidence the plaintiff would need to produce to show either retention or reacquisition.

**The limit on inference.** RSAF must not state, as a fact, that a receivable was transferred simply because the program exists and the account was eligible. That conclusion requires account-specific proof, not inference from program structure. RSAF must identify the inference as an inference and must make clear that the burden of proof remains with the plaintiff.

The correct formulation:

> Plaintiff's public documents describe a program under which eligible receivables of the type at issue may be transferred. Plaintiff has not produced account-specific evidence showing either that this receivable was excluded from the program or that it was reacquired after being transferred. Under the plaintiff's burden of proof, the absence of such evidence is an evidentiary gap bearing on the standing element.

Not:

> Because the program exists and the account was eligible, the receivable was transferred.

Whether governing law recognizes a broader evidentiary inference from a bank's documented routine securitization practice — for example, a rebuttable presumption that eligible receivables were transferred — is an unresolved legal question that RSAF does not presently treat as settled. For the analysis of that question, see Chapter 22 (Routine Securitization Practice) and OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md (OLQ-001). Until that question is resolved by binding authority, RSAF requires account-specific documentation rather than a routine-practice inference.

---

## 8.8 Common Analytical Errors

**Error 1: Treating structural evidence as transaction evidence.** The existence of a TSA does not prove that this receivable was transferred under it. The TSA describes the mechanism. An executed assignment schedule documents a specific transfer.

**Error 2: Treating the absence of exclusion as proof of inclusion.** The fact that no document shows the receivable was excluded from the program does not prove it was included. Absence of one document is not positive evidence of anything.

**Error 3: Treating servicing as ownership.** A servicer that appears in account records, statements, or payment processing records is not thereby shown to own the receivable. Servicing and ownership are separate.

**Error 4: Treating program documentation as account-specific proof.** A prospectus supplement describing how the program works does not describe what happened to the defendant's account.

**Error 5: Conflating authentication with proof of ownership.** A records custodian affidavit that authenticates account statements does not thereby prove the plaintiff owns the receivable. Authentication is the threshold question. Ownership is a separate question that the authenticated records may or may not address.

---

## 8.9 Applying the Distinction

In each RSAF analysis, after the evidence inventory and classification steps, RSAF must separately list the program-level evidence and the account-level evidence the plaintiff has produced.

For each piece of program-level evidence, RSAF identifies:
- what the document establishes about the program;
- what account-specific documentation the document contemplates should exist at the relevant transfer step.

For each piece of account-level evidence, RSAF identifies:
- what specific event or transfer it documents;
- whether it specifically identifies the defendant's account or receivable;
- whether it has been authenticated;
- whether it is linked to an executed transfer instrument.

The comparison between the required account-level evidence (identified using the program documents) and the produced account-level evidence (the evidentiary record) defines the evidentiary gap or, if the record is complete, the basis for the conclusion that the plaintiff has met its burden.

---

## Judge's Checklist

**On program-level evidence:**

- [ ] What program-level documents has the plaintiff produced (prospectus, TSA, PSA, RPA)?
- [ ] Do those documents identify what account-specific proof should exist at each transfer step?
- [ ] Has the plaintiff produced that account-specific proof?

**On account-level evidence:**

- [ ] Does any produced document specifically identify the defendant's account or receivable?
- [ ] Is it tied to an executed transfer instrument?
- [ ] Has it been authenticated?

**The critical question:**

- [ ] Is the plaintiff relying on program-level evidence alone to establish that this specific receivable was transferred or retained?
- [ ] If yes: program-level evidence defines the plaintiff's own transfer framework. It does not prove that the defendant's specific receivable was handled in accordance with that framework.

**On the "plaintiff's own framework" principle:**

- [ ] The plaintiff's own governing agreements specify what documentation a transfer requires. Has the plaintiff produced that documentation?
- [ ] If the plaintiff's governing documents require an executed account schedule for each transfer and the plaintiff has not produced one: the plaintiff has not met its own stated standard.

**On the routine practice open question:**

- [ ] Is the plaintiff arguing that its documented routine practice of transferring eligible receivables creates an inference of transfer for this receivable?
- [ ] That question is currently unresolved under Virginia and Fourth Circuit law. See Chapter 22 and OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md (OLQ-001) before treating this as settled.
