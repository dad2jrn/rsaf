# 11 — Document Standards

## 11.1 Purpose

RSAF-generated litigation documents must be formatted and written to function in the actual forum — General District Court, Circuit Court, or arbitration. This chapter sets standards for each document type that RSAF may be asked to produce.

**RSAF does not draft documents unless explicitly requested.** When the user requests a document, these standards govern its form and content.

---

## 11.2 General Standards Applicable to All Documents

**Numbered paragraphs.** Court filings use numbered paragraphs throughout. Arbitration submissions follow similar conventions. Number every factual and legal paragraph.

**Caption.** Every court filing includes the full case caption: court name, parties, case number, and document title. Match the format of the jurisdiction's standard forms.

**Defined terms.** Introduce a defined term the first time it appears: "Bank of America, N.A. ('BANA')." Use the defined abbreviation consistently thereafter. Do not redefine.

**Technical terminology.** Define securitization terms before using them. The judge may not know what a master trust, depositor, or collateral certificate is. See Chapter 04 (Judicial Communication).

**No fabricated citations.** Every case, statute, and rule cited in a court-facing document must be real, verified, and accurately characterized. See Chapter 12 (Citation Integrity). A document with a fabricated citation is worse than a document with no citation.

**No speculative facts.** RSAF does not allege, assert, or assume facts not established by the record. See CLAUDE.md.

**Signature block.** Every filed document includes a signature block with: name, address, telephone number, and email address. If the user is pro se, mark it "Pro Se."

**Certificate of service.** Include a certificate of service on every document required to be served on opposing counsel, consistent with local rules.

## 11.3 Answer and Affirmative Defenses

**When to use.** The defendant's response to a complaint, warrant in debt, or statement of claim. Required to preserve defenses; failure to plead an affirmative defense may constitute waiver under applicable procedural rules.

**Structure:**

1. Introduction — identifies the document, the party, and the matter.
2. Admissions and denials — a paragraph-by-paragraph response to every numbered allegation in the complaint. Use "admits," "denies," or "denies for lack of sufficient information to form a belief" for each allegation. Do not use "admitted in part and denied in part" without specificity.
3. Affirmative defenses — separately numbered. Each defense must state the legal basis and, to the extent known, the factual predicate.

**Affirmative defenses relevant to RSAF cases:**

- *Lack of standing / failure to prove present right to enforce.* The plaintiff has not produced competent evidence establishing its present legal right to enforce the receivable. State this as both a standing defense and a failure-of-proof defense. See Chapter 06.
- *Failure to state a claim on which relief can be granted.* Available where the complaint does not adequately allege each element of the asserted claim.
- *Statute of limitations.* State the applicable limitation period, the date the cause of action accrued, and that the claim is time-barred. See Chapter 13, § 13.8.
- *Lack of privity / plaintiff is not the real party in interest.* The plaintiff is not the holder of the right being enforced.
- *Business records exception not satisfied.* Where apparent on the face of the complaint or the attached exhibits, challenge the foundation for any records relied upon.
- *Failure of consideration.* Available in appropriate factual circumstances.
- *Compulsory arbitration.* Where the governing cardmember agreement contains a binding arbitration clause, raise the arbitration defense and request a stay or dismissal pending arbitration.

**Do not include kitchen-sink defenses.** Each affirmative defense must be supported by a factual basis available at the time of filing. Defenses that require additional investigation may be listed as "reserved" only if local practice permits.

## 11.4 Motion to Dismiss (Demurrer in Virginia)

**Virginia practice note.** Virginia uses a "demurrer" in lieu of a Rule 12(b)(6) motion to dismiss. A demurrer challenges whether the complaint states a cause of action on its face, taking all well-pleaded facts as true. In General District Court, procedural practice is simplified; challenges to the complaint's sufficiency may be raised differently. Verify applicable procedure before filing.

**Grounds relevant to RSAF cases:**

- The complaint does not allege facts sufficient to show the plaintiff's present right to enforce the receivable.
- The complaint fails to allege the elements of the asserted claim.
- The complaint is barred on its face by the statute of limitations.

**Structure:**

1. Introduction — identifies the filing party, the document, and the relief requested.
2. Factual background — limited to what the complaint itself alleges; no outside facts at the pleading stage.
3. Standard of review — state the applicable standard.
4. Argument — separately headed sections for each ground.
5. Conclusion — states the precise relief requested (dismissal with or without prejudice, leave to amend, etc.).

## 11.5 Motion for Summary Judgment / Summary Judgment Opposition

**When to use.** After the close of discovery, when one party contends there is no genuine dispute of material fact and it is entitled to judgment as a matter of law.

**For the defendant (opposition or cross-motion):**

- A motion attacking the plaintiff's standing at the summary judgment stage should frame the challenge in two ways: (1) lack of standing as a matter of law on the evidence presented; and (2) failure of proof on the ownership element, because the plaintiff has not produced admissible account-specific evidence sufficient to establish the element.
- Attach supporting evidence in admissible form.
- Identify the specific evidentiary gaps: which link in the chain lacks documentation, why that documentation is required, and why the plaintiff's submitted evidence does not fill the gap.

**Structure:**

1. Introduction.
2. Statement of undisputed material facts (or statement of disputed material facts in opposition).
3. Standard of review.
4. Argument — organized by element and theory.
5. Conclusion.

## 11.6 Discovery Requests

RSAF may be asked to draft discovery requests targeting chain-of-title documentation. These are not court filings but must be formatted as formal discovery.

**Interrogatories** — numbered questions. Each interrogatory must ask a single question or a reasonably related set of questions. Virginia General District Court does not use formal interrogatories in the same way as Circuit Court; verify local rules.

**Requests for Production** — request specific categories of documents. In RSAF cases, requests should target:

- The Receivables Purchase Agreement covering the defendant's account.
- The Pooling and Servicing Agreement (or Transfer and Servicing Agreement) covering the defendant's account.
- The account schedule, collateral certificate, or supplement that identifies the defendant's account as included in any transfer.
- The removal notice, removal schedule, or trustee document evidencing removal of the defendant's receivable from any securitization trust.
- The reassignment instrument by which the trust returned the receivable.
- Every bill of sale, assignment, or agreement by which the receivable was transferred from the originating bank to the current plaintiff.
- The cardmember agreement governing the defendant's account.
- The arbitration agreement, if applicable.
- The complete payment and transaction history for the defendant's account.
- Any affidavit, declaration, or certification the plaintiff intends to offer in evidence.

**Requests for Admission** — may be used to establish the absence of documentation. Example: "Admit that Plaintiff has not produced an account schedule identifying Defendant's account as included in any transfer from [Bank] to [Transferor]."

## 11.7 Arbitration Submissions

Where proceedings are before the American Arbitration Association or another arbitration administrator, the formal rules of evidence do not apply with the same force. However, chain-of-title and burden-of-proof arguments remain fully available as merits arguments.

**Statement of defense / answering statement.** The defendant's initial submission in arbitration. Should raise all defenses, including failure to prove ownership, in plain terms accessible to a non-judge arbitrator.

**Evidentiary submissions.** Even where formal rules of evidence do not apply, RSAF should organize documentary submissions with explanations of what each document is and what it does or does not prove.

**Arbitrator education.** Because arbitrators may be less familiar with securitization structure than judges, the teach-before-arguing principle (Chapter 04) is especially important in arbitration submissions.

## 11.8 Formatting Rules

| Item | Standard |
|---|---|
| Font | Times New Roman 12-point or equivalent (verify local rules) |
| Margins | 1 inch (verify local rules) |
| Line spacing | Double-spaced for pleadings; single-spaced for exhibits |
| Headers | Bold, numbered, consistent hierarchy |
| Paragraph numbers | Arabic numerals; reset by section if the document uses section headings |
| Citations | Parallel citation format consistent with Virginia practice |
| Exhibits | Labeled A, B, C, etc.; attached in the order first referenced in text |

Verify local court rules before filing. Formatting requirements vary by court and may be posted on the court's website or in the clerk's office.

---

## 11.9 Verification Requirements

Some Virginia filings require verification — a sworn statement by the filing party that the contents are true. General District Court warrant-in-debt proceedings and some affirmative defenses may require verification. Verify applicable requirements before finalizing any document.

A verification is not a substitute for admissible evidence. A defendant's verification that the plaintiff has not produced documentation is not the same as admissible evidence establishing the absence of documentation. Develop the evidentiary record separately from the verification.
