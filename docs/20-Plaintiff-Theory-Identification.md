# 20 — Plaintiff Theory Identification

## 20.1 Purpose

Before evaluating whether a plaintiff has produced sufficient evidence, RSAF must identify what the plaintiff is claiming. The plaintiff's enforcement theory determines which elements must be proved, what documents would establish those elements, and what a court is actually being asked to decide.

Skipping this step creates a structural error that compounds throughout the analysis. Evidence that is relevant to one enforcement theory may be irrelevant to another. A chain-of-title gap that defeats a reacquisition theory is harmless if the plaintiff never asserted that theory. An authentication deficiency that matters in a breach-of-contract claim may be evaluated differently under an account-stated theory. The evidence must be evaluated against the claim, not in the abstract.

RSAF identifies the plaintiff's enforcement theory as one of the earliest analytical steps — immediately after inventorying the evidence and classifying it by type, and before mapping the transfer path or determining what proof is required. The theory tells RSAF what path to map and what proof to require.

---

## 20.2 The Core Distinction: Present Legal Right to Enforce

RSAF's central question is not whether the plaintiff owns the debt. It is whether the plaintiff has produced legally sufficient evidence establishing its present legal right to enforce the specific receivable under the governing law.

Ownership is one way a plaintiff may establish that right. It is not the only way. A servicer, agent, or authorized representative may assert enforcement rights without claiming ownership. An assignee may claim enforcement rights without claiming to be the original owner. Different theories require different proof. Different theories raise different legal questions.

RSAF must not assume the plaintiff is asserting an ownership theory unless the record supports that characterization. The analysis begins by asking what the plaintiff has actually claimed.

---

## 20.3 How to Identify the Plaintiff's Theory

The plaintiff's enforcement theory is typically found in:

- the complaint or warrant in debt, which identifies the cause of action and the plaintiff's claimed relationship to the receivable;
- the plaintiff's affidavits, which often characterize the plaintiff as "owner," "holder," "servicer," "assignee," or "successor";
- the plaintiff's governing documents attached to the complaint or produced in discovery;
- the plaintiff's discovery responses identifying its basis for suit.

Where the complaint says "Plaintiff purchased this account" or "Plaintiff is the assignee of this account," the plaintiff is asserting an assignee theory. Where the complaint says "Plaintiff is the original creditor and this account was never sold," the plaintiff is asserting a retention theory. Where the plaintiff is identified as a servicer suing on behalf of the trust, the theory is a servicer-authority theory.

RSAF must read these characterizations carefully and must not substitute its own assumption about which theory is more common or more plausible. The plaintiff chooses its theory. RSAF evaluates it.

## 20.4 Possible Enforcement Theories

### Theory 1: Plaintiff Originated and Retained the Receivable

**Description.** The plaintiff contends it is the original creditor — the bank or entity that issued the credit card and extended credit — and that it has held the receivable continuously from origination through the date of suit. Under this theory, the plaintiff never transferred the receivable into any securitization structure or to any third party.

**What plaintiff must prove.**

- That it is, in fact, the original creditor on the account.
- That it has held the receivable continuously without transfer.
- That the receivable was not transferred as part of any securitization program or to any third-party purchaser.
- The amount of the claimed balance.
- That the defendant defaulted.

**Governing legal concepts.** Origination and retention do not require a chain-of-title analysis in the traditional sense, but they require affirmative evidence of continuous ownership. The plaintiff must establish that it is the party from whom the credit was extended, and that it still holds the right to enforce.

**Governing documents.** The cardmember agreement (showing the plaintiff as the issuing bank), the account-opening records, and — critically — evidence that the account was not included in any securitization program, excluded from any pool, or transferred to a trustee or transferor entity.

**Documentary evidence supporting this theory.**

- Original cardmember agreement identifying the plaintiff as issuer.
- Account records identifying the plaintiff as original creditor throughout the account's history.
- Evidence that the account was not included in any trust pool — for example, documentation that the account was excluded from a securitization program, or absence from any trust schedule.
- Program-level documents confirming that not all accounts in the plaintiff's portfolio were transferred, and that accounts meeting certain criteria were retained.

**Documentary evidence that would be insufficient.**

- Monthly statements generated by the plaintiff as servicer — statements do not prove whether the plaintiff is acting as owner or servicer.
- The plaintiff's own characterization in the complaint that it "owns" the account.
- Program-level documents describing a securitization program in general terms without identifying whether this account was included or excluded.
- A records custodian affidavit asserting ownership without identifying the basis for that assertion.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- If the plaintiff's securitization program routinely transferred eligible receivables, how does the plaintiff establish that this particular receivable was excluded or retained?
- Has the plaintiff identified any document specifically excluding this account from the transfer program?
- Is the plaintiff appearing in account records as owner or as servicer? Has the plaintiff explained the difference?
- If the plaintiff's public disclosures describe a comprehensive transfer program, what affirmative evidence shows this receivable was not part of it?

**Common evidentiary weaknesses.**

The retention theory is often asserted without account-specific exclusion documentation. A bank may contend that it retained a receivable simply because it continues to appear in account records — without distinguishing its ownership role from its servicing role. The same records are generated whether the bank owns the receivable outright or services it on behalf of a trust.

### Theory 2: Plaintiff Transferred the Receivable and Later Reacquired It

**Description.** The plaintiff contends that the receivable was transferred into a securitization structure but was subsequently removed from the trust and returned to the plaintiff — through the removal and reassignment procedures in the governing agreements — before suit was filed.

**What plaintiff must prove.**

- That the receivable was transferred to a trust or transferor entity.
- That the receivable was removed from the trust, identifying the provision that authorized removal and the conditions triggering it.
- That the receivable was reassigned to the plaintiff (or to an entity in the chain that then transferred it to the plaintiff).
- That the plaintiff currently holds the right to enforce.
- The amount of the claimed balance.
- That the defendant defaulted.

**Governing legal concepts.** The reacquisition theory requires proof of both halves of the transaction: transfer in, and return. Each half must be documented by account-specific evidence. The governing agreements define the procedures that must be followed for removal and reassignment, and the documentation those procedures generate.

**Governing documents.** The Receivables Purchase Agreement (RPA), Transfer and Servicing Agreement (TSA) or Pooling and Servicing Agreement (PSA) — including the removal and reassignment provisions — and any executed reassignment instruments.

**Documentary evidence supporting this theory.**

- Account schedules identifying the defendant's account as included in a transfer to the trust.
- PSA or TSA provisions specifying the removal trigger applicable to this receivable (e.g., ineligibility provisions, charge-off provisions, breach of representation provisions).
- Account-specific removal notice or removal schedule identifying this receivable.
- Executed reassignment instrument identifying this receivable as reassigned to the plaintiff.
- If the plaintiff is a successor purchaser: bill of sale with account schedule identifying the defendant's account.
- Authentication of each document by a competent foundation witness.

**Documentary evidence that would be insufficient.**

- The PSA or TSA alone, without account-specific removal and reassignment documentation.
- A charge-off record treated as if it constitutes removal documentation.
- A custodian affidavit asserting that the receivable was removed and reassigned without identifying the removal document, the triggering provision, or the reassignment instrument.
- Program-level descriptions of removal and reassignment procedures without corresponding account-level documentation.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- What document reflects the removal of this specific receivable from the trust?
- What provision of the governing agreement authorized the removal?
- Who executed the reassignment, and under what authority?
- Does the reassignment instrument identify this defendant's account?
- If the plaintiff is a subsequent purchaser, what document conveys the receivable from the post-removal holder to the plaintiff?

**Common evidentiary weaknesses.**

The reacquisition chain frequently breaks at the removal or reassignment link. Plaintiffs often produce program-level documents describing how removal is supposed to work, and charge-off records reflecting the accounting event, without producing the account-specific removal notice or executed reassignment instrument required by the governing agreements. The result is a structural map and an accounting entry, but no account-specific documentation of the legal event.

### Theory 3: Plaintiff Retained Legal Title While Beneficial or Economic Interests Were Transferred

**Description.** The plaintiff contends that it retained legal title to the receivable throughout, even though beneficial or economic interests were transferred to investors or a trust. Under this theory, the plaintiff argues it was always the legal owner and that the securitization structure transferred only economic or beneficial interests, not the legal right to enforce.

**What plaintiff must prove.**

- That the governing agreements and applicable law support a distinction between legal title and beneficial interest.
- That legal title did not pass under the applicable transfer.
- That the plaintiff, as legal title holder, has the present right to enforce.

**Governing legal concepts.** This theory depends on the characterization of the securitization transfers under applicable law — specifically whether the transfer of receivables into the trust constituted a transfer of legal title or only a pledge or assignment of economic interests. This analysis requires examination of the governing agreements, applicable UCC Article 9 provisions, and whether the transfer constituted a "true sale" sufficient to vest legal title in the trust.

The theory conflicts, in some respects, with the true-sale treatment that many securitization programs are structured to achieve. If the bank retained legal title, the transfer may not qualify as a true sale, which has significant accounting and regulatory implications.

**Governing documents.** The RPA, PSA, or TSA — specifically the transfer provisions and any representations as to the nature of the conveyance. SEC filings disclosing how the bank accounts for the transfers. Applicable state law on transfer of accounts.

**Documentary evidence supporting this theory.**

- Governing agreements explicitly preserving legal title in the originating bank while conveying economic interests.
- Legal opinions or accounting disclosures characterizing the transfers accordingly.
- UCC filings consistent with a secured-borrowing rather than a true-sale characterization (though this creates accounting complications the bank may have sought to avoid).

**Documentary evidence that would be insufficient.**

- General program descriptions of a securitization that describe the transfers as sales.
- SEC filings disclosing true-sale treatment, which would be inconsistent with a retained-legal-title theory.
- Custodian affidavits asserting legal title without identifying the legal basis for that assertion.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- What specific provision of the governing agreements preserves legal title in the plaintiff?
- Is this characterization consistent with how the plaintiff disclosed the transfers in its SEC filings?
- If the plaintiff now claims legal title, what does that imply about the "true sale" treatment it disclosed to the SEC and its investors?
- Under applicable UCC Article 9, does the plaintiff's asserted characterization constitute a sale or a secured borrowing?

**Common evidentiary weaknesses.**

This theory faces tension with the disclosures most national bank securitization programs make to the SEC. Banks have typically sought true-sale treatment for accounting and regulatory purposes. A plaintiff that disclosed true-sale treatment to the SEC while asserting in litigation that it retained legal title may face questions about consistency. RSAF must note this tension without assuming either characterization is necessarily correct; the analysis requires examination of the specific governing documents and applicable law.

### Theory 4: Plaintiff Sues as Servicer

**Description.** The plaintiff contends that it has the right to enforce the receivable not as owner but as servicer, acting under a servicing agreement that grants the servicer authority to collect on behalf of the trust or investors.

**What plaintiff must prove.**

- The existence of a valid servicing agreement authorizing the servicer to sue in its own name.
- That the agreement covers this receivable.
- That the plaintiff is, in fact, acting as servicer under that agreement.
- That the servicing agreement grants the specific right to initiate and maintain collection litigation.

**Governing legal concepts.** A servicer's authority to sue is a contractual question. Not every servicing agreement grants the right to commence litigation in the servicer's own name. Many servicing agreements grant the right to collect on behalf of the trust but require the trust (or trustee) to be named in litigation. The difference between suing "on behalf of" the trust and suing in the servicer's own name matters procedurally and substantively.

**Governing documents.** The servicing agreement — which is typically a section of the PSA or TSA — including the provisions specifying the servicer's collection authority, whether litigation may be commenced in the servicer's name, and what authorization is required.

**Documentary evidence supporting this theory.**

- An executed servicing agreement identifying the plaintiff as servicer.
- A provision within that agreement specifically authorizing the servicer to commence collection litigation in its own name.
- Account-specific evidence identifying the defendant's receivable as within the scope of the servicing agreement.
- Authorization or direction from the trust or trustee to commence this action, if required by the governing documents.

**Documentary evidence that would be insufficient.**

- Monthly statements showing the plaintiff managed the account.
- General evidence that the plaintiff is a servicer for other accounts.
- A servicing agreement that authorizes the servicer to "collect" without expressly authorizing litigation in the servicer's name.
- An affidavit asserting authority to sue without identifying the specific contractual provision granting that authority.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- Does the servicing agreement specifically authorize suit in the servicer's name, or does it authorize collection more generally?
- If the servicing agreement authorizes suit in the servicer's name, what is the trust's or trustee's role in that authorization?
- Has the servicer produced the relevant provisions of the servicing agreement as evidence?
- If the trust — not the servicer — holds the legal right to enforce, is the trust a necessary party?

**Common evidentiary weaknesses.**

The servicer theory requires a careful reading of the servicing agreement's litigation authority provisions. These provisions vary across securitization programs. Some explicitly grant the right to sue in the servicer's name; others require trust or trustee authorization; others contemplate litigation only in the trust's name. A plaintiff asserting servicer authority must produce the specific contractual provision, not a general characterization.

### Theory 5: Plaintiff Sues as Agent or Authorized Representative

**Description.** The plaintiff contends that it has authority to enforce the receivable as agent or authorized representative of the owner — typically the trust or a trustee — rather than in its own capacity as owner or servicer.

**What plaintiff must prove.**

- The existence of an agency relationship or formal authorization.
- The scope of that authorization, including authority to commence litigation.
- The identity of the principal (the owner of the receivable).
- That the plaintiff is acting within the scope of the authorization in commencing this action.

**Governing legal concepts.** An agent may sue on behalf of a principal if the agency relationship exists and the agent has authority to commence the action. The existence and scope of the agency are questions of fact governed by the applicable agreement and the governing law on agency. In some jurisdictions, the agent must identify the principal; in others, an agent may sue in its own name if authorized.

**Governing documents.** The power of attorney, authorization agreement, or agency agreement granting the right to act. The governing PSA, TSA, or trust agreement that may also address authorized parties.

**Documentary evidence supporting this theory.**

- An executed power of attorney or authorization agreement identifying the plaintiff as agent.
- A provision of the governing agreement authorizing the servicer or another party to act as agent.
- Identification of the principal holding the receivable.
- Evidence that the authorization covers this specific type of action.

**Documentary evidence that would be insufficient.**

- General assertions that the plaintiff acts on behalf of the trust.
- A servicing agreement without a specific agency or litigation-authority provision.
- Conclusory statements in an affidavit that the plaintiff is "authorized" to collect without identifying the source of that authorization.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- Who is the principal in this action? Is the principal identified?
- What document creates the agency relationship?
- Does the authorizing document specifically grant the right to commence litigation?
- Has the agent established that the principal holds the legal right to enforce the receivable?

**Common evidentiary weaknesses.**

This theory requires both proof of the agency relationship and proof that the principal has the right to enforce. If the principal does not hold the receivable, the agent's authority is irrelevant. Plaintiffs asserting this theory often skip the second step.

### Theory 6: Plaintiff Sues as Assignee

**Description.** The plaintiff contends that it acquired the receivable by assignment from a prior holder, and that the assignment conveys the present right to enforce. This is the most common theory among debt buyers — entities that purchase portfolios of charged-off accounts.

**What plaintiff must prove.**

- The existence of a valid assignment from the prior holder.
- That the assignment conveys the specific receivable at issue.
- That the assignor held the right to assign — that is, that the assignor itself held the right to enforce, either as originator, reacquirer, or prior assignee.
- An unbroken chain of title from the originating creditor to the plaintiff through each assignment.
- The amount owed.
- That the defendant defaulted.

**Governing legal concepts.** An assignee acquires no greater rights than the assignor possessed. If the assignor did not hold the right to enforce — because the receivable was in a trust and had not been removed and reassigned — the assignment conveys nothing. The chain of title must extend from the entity that originally held the right to enforce to the current plaintiff, with each link documented.

**Governing documents.** Every bill of sale, assignment agreement, and account schedule in the chain from the originating creditor to the plaintiff. The originating creditor's transfer documents (RPA, PSA), removal and reassignment documentation if applicable, and the assignment from each intermediate holder.

**Documentary evidence supporting this theory.**

- Executed bills of sale or assignment agreements at each step in the chain.
- Account schedules identifying the defendant's account as included in each transfer.
- Documentation showing that the originating creditor held (or reacquired) the right to assign before the first assignment in the chain.
- Authentication of each document.

**Documentary evidence that would be insufficient.**

- A bill of sale without an account schedule identifying this account.
- A generic assignment asserting transfer of "all charged-off accounts" without identifying this account.
- A custodian affidavit asserting the plaintiff owns the account without producing the underlying assignment documents.
- A chain with a missing link — for example, an assignment from Bank A to Assignee C without showing how Assignee C received the right from Bank A if Bank B was an intermediate holder.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- Where does the chain of title begin?
- Has the plaintiff produced an executed assignment at each step in the chain?
- Does each assignment identify this defendant's account — either directly or by reference to a schedule?
- Does the account schedule containing the defendant's account number correspond to the executed assignment, or were they produced separately without connecting them?
- Has the assignor at each step established its own right to assign before purporting to convey that right?

**Common evidentiary weaknesses.**

The bill of sale without a corresponding account schedule is the most common deficiency in assignee-theory cases. The bill of sale establishes that a transfer occurred; the schedule establishes that this particular account was included. Both are required. Producing one without the other leaves the specific transfer of this receivable undocumented.

A related weakness is the missing intermediate link. If the originating bank transferred a receivable into a trust, the trust must have removed and reassigned it before the bank could assign it to anyone. Where that intermediate step is not documented, the chain is broken at the point before the first assignment to a third party.

### Theory 7: Plaintiff's Theory Is Unclear

**Description.** The plaintiff has not made clear which enforcement theory it is advancing, or has asserted multiple inconsistent theories without clearly distinguishing them.

**What this means for analysis.**

An unclear theory is itself a significant evidentiary problem. If RSAF cannot identify the theory, a judge likely cannot either. A plaintiff that cannot articulate the basis of its claimed enforcement right has not carried its burden at the threshold level.

When the theory is unclear, RSAF must:

1. State that the theory cannot be identified from the pleadings and evidence.
2. Evaluate the evidence under each plausible theory.
3. Identify which theories the evidence might support and which it cannot.
4. Note that the plaintiff has not carried its burden if it cannot establish its theory under any applicable standard.

**Common indicators of an unclear theory.**

- The complaint characterizes the plaintiff as "owner" without identifying how it acquired that status.
- The affidavit asserts the plaintiff "is entitled to collect" without identifying the legal basis.
- The plaintiff produces program-level documents and account statements without indicating whether it is claiming as originator, reacquirer, or assignee.
- The plaintiff shifts its characterization between the complaint, discovery, and trial.

**Questions a judge should ask.**

- What specific legal basis does the plaintiff assert for its right to enforce?
- How did the plaintiff acquire that right?
- What evidence in the record supports that specific theory?

## 20.5 Theory Identification and the Analysis That Follows

Once RSAF has identified the plaintiff's enforcement theory, each subsequent analytical step is calibrated to that theory.

The transfer path mapped under Section 5.12 of the Legal Methodology reflects the specific path the plaintiff has claimed, not a generic map of all possible paths.

The required proof identified under Section 5.13 reflects what must be proven under the identified theory, not a generic list of everything that might be relevant.

The evidentiary gaps identified under Section 5.16 are gaps relative to the plaintiff's stated theory, not failures under theories the plaintiff has not advanced.

**If the plaintiff's theory changes mid-litigation,** RSAF must note the change and evaluate whether the change is supported by the evidence. A plaintiff that switches from a retention theory to a reacquisition theory after discovery has implicitly acknowledged that the retention theory may not be supported. That acknowledgment may itself be significant.

---

## 20.6 What RSAF Does Not Do

RSAF does not choose a theory for the plaintiff.

RSAF does not assume that the most favorable theory for the plaintiff is the one the plaintiff has asserted.

RSAF does not assume that a theory is credible merely because it is possible.

RSAF evaluates the theory the plaintiff has advanced, measures the evidence against the requirements of that theory, and identifies the evidentiary record that results. The legal conclusion — whether the plaintiff has met its burden — follows from that evaluation.
