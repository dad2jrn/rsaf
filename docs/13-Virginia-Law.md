# 13 — Virginia Law

## 13.1 Scope and Research Rule

This chapter surveys Virginia law relevant to consumer credit-card receivable litigation. Its purpose is to identify the governing legal framework, the relevant statutory provisions, and the analytical questions RSAF must address in Virginia proceedings.

**Research Rule.** This chapter identifies areas of law and general principles. Before citing any statute, rule, or case in a court-facing document, verify the current text, the exact citation, and the precise holding. Law changes. This chapter is a research guide, not a primary source.

---

## 13.2 Virginia Court Structure

Consumer credit-card collection actions in Virginia may proceed in General District Court or Circuit Court, depending on the amount in controversy and procedural posture.

**General District Court.** The General District Court hears civil cases up to a statutory jurisdictional limit established by the Virginia Code. See Va. Code § 16.1-77. General District Court proceedings are governed by simplified procedure; the Virginia Rules of Evidence apply in modified form. Appeals from General District Court judgments proceed to Circuit Court as trials de novo.

**Circuit Court.** The Circuit Court is a court of general jurisdiction. It has jurisdiction over civil cases without a jurisdictional cap. Appeals from General District Court provide a fresh start in Circuit Court. The Virginia Rules of Evidence and Virginia Rules of Supreme Court apply in Circuit Court proceedings.

**Arbitration.** Where the cardmember agreement includes an arbitration clause, proceedings may proceed before an arbitration administrator such as the American Arbitration Association under its Consumer Arbitration Rules. The Federal Arbitration Act governs the enforceability of the arbitration agreement.

---

## 13.3 Pleading Standards

Virginia practice in General District Court is initiated by a Warrant in Debt or Warrant in Detinue. Circuit Court practice uses a Complaint governed by the Virginia Rules of Supreme Court.

The plaintiff must plead each claim with sufficient particularity to put the defendant on notice of the factual and legal basis of the claim. In a debt collection action, this ordinarily requires the plaintiff to identify the underlying account, the basis for the claimed balance, and its right to enforce.

A defendant who fails to raise certain affirmative defenses at the appropriate time may waive them. RSAF must identify which defenses are subject to waiver and ensure they are preserved in the responsive pleading.

---

## 13.4 Virginia Law on Standing and Capacity to Sue

A plaintiff suing in Virginia must have standing — a legally cognizable interest in the subject matter of the litigation. Where the plaintiff claims to own a receivable, its ownership is part of its standing to enforce.

Virginia courts apply substantive Virginia law to the question of whether a party has the legal right to maintain a civil action, subject to any applicable choice-of-law provisions.

In the context of assigned debts and purchased receivables, Virginia courts may examine whether the plaintiff has produced competent evidence of an assignment or transfer that would entitle it to sue in its own name.

**Case authority.** Virginia case law on the sufficiency of evidence to establish ownership of an assigned consumer receivable is developing. RSAF identifies this as an active research area. Before citing any Virginia case on ownership or chain-of-title sufficiency, verify the exact citation, the court, the year, the procedural posture, and the holding. Pay particular attention to whether the decision is binding on the court before which the current case is pending.

## 13.5 Virginia Evidence Rules Governing Business Records

Virginia adopted a codified set of evidence rules effective July 1, 2012. The Virginia Rules of Evidence appear in the Rules of the Supreme Court of Virginia.

**Business records.** Rule 2:803(6) of the Virginia Rules of Evidence codifies the business records exception to the hearsay rule. Under this rule, records of a regularly conducted business activity may be admitted if:

- the record was made at or near the time of the event recorded;
- the record was made by someone with knowledge of the event, or from information transmitted by such a person;
- the record was kept in the course of a regularly conducted business activity;
- creating the record was a regular practice of that business activity;
- the foundation is established by testimony of the custodian or other qualified witness, or by certification where permitted.

The rule excludes records where the source of information or the method or circumstances of preparation indicate a lack of trustworthiness.

**Verify:** Before citing Rule 2:803(6) in any court-facing document, verify the current text of the Rule as in effect in the forum court.

**Acquired records.** Virginia courts may scrutinize the business-records foundation for documents that were created by one entity and acquired by another. A custodian employed by the current plaintiff who did not work for the originating creditor may lack personal knowledge necessary to authenticate the originating creditor's records without additional foundation.

**Custodian affidavits.** Where records are authenticated by affidavit rather than live testimony, the affidavit must satisfy the foundational requirements of the applicable rule. Authentication through affidavit does not reduce the substantive evidentiary requirements.

## 13.6 Virginia UCC Article 9: Sales of Accounts and Payment Intangibles

Virginia has adopted Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code. The relevant Virginia Code sections appear as Va. Code § 8.9A et seq.

**Scope.** Va. Code § 8.9A-109 brings within the scope of Article 9 the sale of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes. Credit-card receivables are generally "accounts" within this definition. The sale of credit-card receivables is therefore governed by Article 9's perfection and priority rules.

**Perfection.** Under Article 9, a sale of accounts is generally perfected by filing a UCC-1 financing statement. Program-level UCC filings may cover pools of receivables transferred to the trust. These filings are structural evidence. They establish perfection of the security interest or ownership claim in the pool as a whole; they do not specifically identify any individual account.

**Account debtor rights.** Va. Code § 8.9A-406 addresses the account debtor's right to receive reasonable proof of assignment before being obligated to pay the assignee. Where the defendant raises this right, the analysis must examine whether adequate proof of assignment was provided and what consequences flow from any deficiency.

**Verify:** Before relying on any specific UCC provision in a court-facing document, verify the current text of the applicable Virginia Code section and confirm it has not been amended.

## 13.7 Contract Claims in Virginia

Where the governing cardmember agreement designates Delaware law (or another state's law) as controlling, the Virginia court applies that choice-of-law provision to interpret and enforce the contract, subject to Virginia public policy exceptions.

Virginia recognizes breach of contract claims requiring the plaintiff to establish existence of a valid contract, breach by the defendant, and damages. Account stated is a recognized theory under Virginia law. RSAF must identify the specific Virginia authorities governing each theory before relying on them.

---

## 13.8 Statute of Limitations

Virginia imposes limitation periods on civil actions. The applicable period for credit-card debt claims may depend on whether the claim sounds in contract or in account stated, and whether it is a written or oral contract.

The limitations period begins to run when the cause of action accrues — typically when the right to sue arises. In a credit-card collection action, this is often related to the date of default or last payment.

A defendant may raise the statute of limitations as an affirmative defense. RSAF must identify the applicable period, calculate when the period began to run, and evaluate whether the claim is timely.

**Research target.** Verify the applicable Virginia limitations periods (Va. Code § 8.01-246 for written contracts and related provisions) before asserting or challenging timeliness. Choice-of-law analysis may affect which state's limitations period applies.
