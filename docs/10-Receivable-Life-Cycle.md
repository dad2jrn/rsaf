# 10 — Receivable Life Cycle

## 10.1 Overview

RSAF analyzes a receivable across its entire life, from the moment it is created to the moment a judgment is entered, arbitration is resolved, or the case is otherwise concluded.

Understanding the life cycle serves two purposes. First, it forces the analysis to follow the chronological path of the receivable rather than jumping to conclusions about current ownership based on documents from a single point in time. Second, it identifies the specific stages at which account-level evidence should have been created — and therefore the specific evidence a plaintiff should be able to produce if its ownership theory is accurate.

The life cycle is not a checklist to be completed in order. It is a map. RSAF uses the map to identify where the receivable was at each critical point and what documentary evidence should correspond to each location.

---

## 10.2 Life-Cycle Stages

```text
Account opened
            ↓
Charges incurred; receivable created
            ↓
Receivable held by originating bank
            ↓
Possible transfer to bankruptcy-remote transferor
(Receivables Purchase Agreement)
            ↓
Possible transfer to issuing trust
(Transfer and Servicing Agreement)
            ↓
Trust holds receivable; servicer administers account
            ↓
Default; account becomes delinquent
            ↓
Charge-off by servicer
            ↓
Possible removal from trust (per governing agreement procedures)
            ↓
Possible reassignment (to transferor, bank, or third party)
            ↓
Collection litigation or arbitration
```

Each transition in this sequence may require documentation under the governing agreements. RSAF must identify each transition, determine whether it occurred (and if so, what documents govern it), and evaluate whether the plaintiff has produced account-specific evidence corresponding to each step.

---

## 10.3 Stage-by-Stage Analysis

### Stage 1: Account Opened

When a credit-card account is opened, the governing agreement — typically the cardmember agreement — is established between the issuing bank and the consumer.

**Documents this stage produces:** The cardmember agreement, credit application, and account-opening records.

**RSAF question:** Does the plaintiff have a governing agreement that establishes the contractual relationship with the defendant?

**Relevance to standing:** The cardmember agreement is foundational to any contract theory. If it is governed by a specific state's law (commonly Delaware for many national bank programs), that choice-of-law provision governs interpretation of the agreement's transfer and enforcement provisions.

---

### Stage 2: Charges Incurred; Receivable Created

Each time the cardholder uses the credit card, charges are incurred and the right to repayment arises. The receivable consists of the principal balance of all unpaid charges, plus accrued interest and fees under the governing agreement.

**Documents this stage produces:** Monthly statements, transaction records, payment histories.

**RSAF question:** Has the plaintiff produced admissible evidence of the balance claimed?

**Relevance to standing:** Servicing records from this stage establish account activity and the claimed balance. They do not establish who presently owns the receivable.

---

### Stage 3: Receivable Held by Originating Bank

Immediately after creation, the receivable is held by the originating bank. Whether it remains there or is transferred depends on the bank's securitization program.

**Documents this stage produces:** No transfer documentation is generated here, by definition. The absence of transfer documentation at this stage is relevant only if the plaintiff contends the receivable was never transferred.

**RSAF question:** Did the receivable remain with the originating bank, or was it transferred into the securitization structure?

**Relevance to standing:** If the plaintiff is the originating bank and contends it never transferred the receivable, it must be prepared to produce account-specific evidence supporting that position when ownership is challenged.

---

### Stage 4: Transfer to Bankruptcy-Remote Transferor (if applicable)

Under many national bank credit-card securitization programs, the originating bank sells pools of eligible receivables to a bankruptcy-remote transferor entity through a Receivables Purchase Agreement.

**Documents this stage produces:** An executed Receivables Purchase Agreement covering the relevant accounts, and typically an account schedule or exhibit identifying the specific receivables transferred. These are program-level documents (the RPA itself) plus account-level documents (the schedule identifying specific accounts).

**RSAF question:** Was the defendant's receivable sold to the transferor? If so, what account-specific documentation evidences that sale?

**Relevance to standing:** If the receivable was transferred at this stage, the originating bank no longer owns it without reacquisition. For the bank to sue as current owner, it must show the receivable returned.

---

### Stage 5: Transfer to Issuing Trust (if applicable)

The transferor entity transfers the purchased receivables to the issuing trust pursuant to a Transfer and Servicing Agreement or Pooling and Servicing Agreement.

**Documents this stage produces:** An executed TSA or PSA covering the relevant accounts, and typically an account schedule or assignment identifying the specific receivables included in the trust.

**RSAF question:** Was the defendant's receivable transferred to the trust? If so, what account-specific documentation evidences that transfer?

**Relevance to standing:** If the receivable entered the trust, the trust holds the beneficial interest in the receivable. Neither the bank nor the transferor can enforce it without reacquisition unless the governing documents provide otherwise.

---

### Stage 6: Trust Holds Receivable; Servicer Administers Account

While the receivable is in the trust, the servicer (often the originating bank) continues to administer the account. Statements are generated, payments are processed, and records are maintained — all by the servicer.

**Documents this stage produces:** Monthly statements, payment records, account notes — all generated by the servicer.

**RSAF question:** Who generates these records, and in what capacity? As owner or as servicer?

**Relevance to standing:** This is where the servicing-ownership confusion most commonly arises. Records generated by the bank during this stage reflect the bank's servicing activities, not its ownership. The bank's appearance in these records does not establish its ownership of the receivable.

---

### Stage 7: Default and Delinquency

When the cardholder stops paying, the account becomes delinquent. Most credit-card programs have specific delinquency triggers that govern how and when the servicer must act.

**Documents this stage produces:** Delinquency notices, payment demand records, internal collection records.

**RSAF question:** When did default occur, and is it documented?

**Relevance to standing:** Default is relevant to the breach element of the plaintiff's claim. It is not by itself relevant to the ownership question. A receivable in default is still owned by whoever held the ownership interest before default.

---

### Stage 8: Charge-Off

After a specified period of non-payment (commonly 180 days for credit-card accounts), the servicer charges off the balance. Charge-off is an accounting event that removes the balance from the bank's income-producing assets and recognizes a loss.

**Documents this stage produces:** Charge-off records, final balance records as of the charge-off date.

**RSAF question:** Was the account charged off? What was the balance at charge-off? Who charged it off, and in what capacity?

**Relevance to standing:** Charge-off does not extinguish the debt or the receivable. A charged-off receivable is still owed; it simply has been written off for accounting purposes. The charge-off event may also trigger removal procedures under the governing agreements.

---

### Stage 9: Possible Removal from Trust (if applicable)

Many securitization programs require or permit the removal of charged-off, ineligible, or defaulted receivables from the trust. The governing agreements specify how removal occurs, what conditions trigger it, who authorizes it, and what documentation is required.

**Documents this stage produces:** Removal notices, account schedules identifying removed receivables, resolutions or certifications authorizing the removal, executed instruments effecting the removal.

**RSAF question:** Was the defendant's receivable removed from the trust? If so, under what provision? What documentation reflects the removal? To whom was the receivable directed upon removal?

**Relevance to standing:** If the receivable was removed from the trust, the question shifts to where it went. The plaintiff must trace the receivable from the trust to its present holder through account-specific documentation.

---

### Stage 10: Possible Reassignment (if applicable)

After removal from the trust, the governing agreements typically specify who receives the reassigned receivable. It may return to the transferor entity, to the originating bank, or be assigned directly to a third-party purchaser.

**Documents this stage produces:** Executed reassignment instruments, account schedules identifying reassigned receivables, certifications from the trustee or servicer.

**RSAF question:** To whom was the receivable reassigned after removal? Is that documented by account-specific evidence? If the plaintiff is a subsequent purchaser or assignee, what additional documents show the chain from the post-removal holder to the plaintiff?

**Relevance to standing:** The reassignment step is often the weakest link in the plaintiff's chain of title. Even where program-level documents describe the reassignment procedures clearly, account-specific evidence must show that those procedures were followed for this receivable.

---

### Stage 11: Collection Litigation or Arbitration

The plaintiff files suit or initiates arbitration. At this point, the plaintiff must establish every element of its claim, including the present legal right to enforce the receivable.

**Documents this stage produces:** Complaint or demand for arbitration, exhibits, discovery responses, affidavits.

**RSAF question:** Has the plaintiff produced account-specific evidence sufficient to establish its present legal right to enforce the receivable, measured against the governing law and the plaintiff's own documentary framework?

---

## 10.4 Retention Theory

If the plaintiff contends it retained the receivable and never transferred it into the securitization structure, RSAF must identify what evidence supports that position.

Retention may be evidenced by:

- account-specific exclusion documentation showing the receivable was removed or excluded from any transfer to the transferor;
- absence of the defendant's account from any trust schedule or eligible receivable pool;
- governing agreement provisions that excluded this type of account or this specific receivable;
- internal records confirming the receivable was held outside the trust throughout the relevant period.

Retention is not established by:

- the plaintiff's characterization in its complaint;
- the existence of the plaintiff's name in servicing records (which may reflect servicing, not ownership);
- the absence of contrary evidence (absence of evidence is not proof of retention).

The burden of showing retention rests with the plaintiff. The plaintiff asserting retention must produce affirmative evidence that the receivable was not transferred.

---

## 10.5 Reacquisition Theory

If the plaintiff contends the receivable was transferred and later returned, RSAF must examine each link in the chain from the trust back to the plaintiff.

A complete reacquisition chain includes:

1. Account-specific documentation of the transfer into the trust (Stage 4 and Stage 5).
2. Account-specific documentation of the removal from the trust (Stage 9).
3. Account-specific documentation of the reassignment to the transferor or bank (Stage 10).
4. If the current plaintiff is not the original bank, account-specific documentation of any subsequent transfer.
5. Authentication of each document by a competent witness.

A partial chain — for example, a removal notice but no executed reassignment — is an incomplete chain. An incomplete chain does not establish the plaintiff's current ownership.

---

## 10.6 Applying the Life Cycle

After mapping the receivable's theoretical life cycle, RSAF must:

1. Identify which theory the plaintiff has advanced (retention or reacquisition).
2. Identify what account-specific documentation each theory requires.
3. Compare that requirement to the documents the plaintiff has actually produced.
4. State the gaps precisely.
5. Assess whether the evidentiary gaps defeat the plaintiff's standing on the current record.

The life cycle map does not create evidence. It tells RSAF where to look and what to look for. The evidentiary record tells RSAF whether the required documents are there.
