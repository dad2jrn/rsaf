# 09. Securitization Primer

Receivable Standing Analysis Framework (RSAF)  
Version: 0.1.0  
Status: Draft

## 09.1 Purpose

This chapter provides a plain-English primer for explaining credit-card receivable securitization to a court.

## 09.2 Account vs. Receivable

A credit-card account is the relationship between the cardholder and the issuing bank.

A receivable is the legal right to receive payment arising from charges, fees, interest, or other obligations associated with that account.

The account and receivable may be treated differently in securitization documents.

## 09.3 Basic Transfer Structure

A simplified credit-card securitization structure may look like this:

```text
Cardholder Account
      │
      ▼
Receivable Created
      │
      ▼
Originating Bank
      │
      ▼
Receivables Purchase Agreement
      │
      ▼
Bankruptcy-Remote Transferor LLC
      │
      ▼
Transfer / Pooling / Servicing Agreement
      │
      ▼
Issuing Trust
      │
      ▼
Investors receive trust distributions
```

## 09.4 Why the Bankruptcy-Remote Entity Matters

A bankruptcy-remote entity is often used to separate receivables from the originating bank's general assets. The structure is designed to support true-sale treatment and protect trust investors from risks associated with the bank's own creditors.

## 09.5 Servicer vs. Owner

The bank may remain servicer even after receivables are transferred. A servicer may send statements, collect payments, maintain records, and administer accounts. Servicing authority does not necessarily equal ownership.

## 09.6 Reacquisition Path

If a receivable is transferred and later returns, RSAF must identify the contractual path for that return.

A simplified structure may look like this:

```text
Issuing Trust
      │
      ▼
Default / Removal Event
      │
      ▼
Transferor LLC
      │
      ▼
Reassignment Document
      │
      ▼
Originating Bank or Plaintiff
```

The key question is whether the plaintiff has produced the executed documents and account-specific schedules required to prove that return path.
