# 09 — Securitization Primer

## 9.1 Purpose

This chapter explains credit-card receivable securitization in plain English for judges, litigants, and AI systems applying RSAF.

## 9.2 What Is a Receivable?

A receivable is a right to payment. In a credit-card context, it is the right to receive payment arising from charges, interest, fees, or other obligations on a credit-card account.

## 9.3 Account vs Receivable

The account is the credit relationship. The receivable is the right to payment generated by that relationship. A bank may service an account while a trust or other entity owns the receivable.

## 9.4 Why Banks Securitize Receivables

Banks may sell pools of receivables into asset-backed securitization structures to obtain funding, manage balance-sheet exposure, and provide investors with interests backed by receivable cash flows.

## 9.5 Typical Transfer Path

```text
Consumer Credit-Card Account
        │
        ▼
Receivable Created
        │
        ▼
Originating Bank
        │
        ▼
Receivables Purchase Agreement
        │
        ▼
Bankruptcy-Remote Transferor LLC
        │
        ▼
Transfer and Servicing Agreement
        │
        ▼
Issuing Trust
        │
        ▼
Investor Interests / Notes
```

## 9.6 Bankruptcy-Remote Transferor

A bankruptcy-remote entity is typically used to separate transferred receivables from the originating bank. It may purchase receivables from the bank and then transfer them to a trust.

## 9.7 True Sale

A true sale is intended to transfer ownership rather than create only a secured loan. RSAF does not assume true-sale treatment from labels alone. It examines the governing documents and applicable law.

## 9.8 Servicing

The servicer administers accounts, collects payments, sends statements, and maintains records. Servicing is not the same as ownership unless the governing documents or account-level evidence establish both.

## 9.9 Reacquisition

If a receivable was transferred away from the bank, the bank must generally show how it reacquired the receivable before suing as owner. The governing agreements often specify the reassignment mechanism and required schedules.

## 9.10 The Judge's Five-Minute Explanation

This case is about the legal right to collect a specific receivable. Monthly statements may show that an account existed and that a balance was claimed. They do not necessarily show who presently owns the right to payment. The bank's public securitization documents describe how receivables are transferred and what documents record those transfers. If the plaintiff claims the right to enforce this receivable, the court must examine whether the plaintiff has produced account-specific evidence proving that right.
