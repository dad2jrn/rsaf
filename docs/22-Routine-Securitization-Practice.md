# 22 — Routine Securitization Practice

## 22.1 Purpose

This chapter presents a legal issue — not a conclusion.

The issue is whether the routine, publicly documented, and comprehensively disclosed practice of transferring eligible credit-card receivables under a securitization program creates any evidentiary inference applicable to a specific receivable, and, if so, what kind of inference, under what conditions, and whether it is rebuttable.

RSAF does not answer this question. The question requires research into governing law that is not yet complete. This chapter explains what the question is, why it matters, what the competing positions are, and what additional research would be required before RSAF could adopt a position.

The chapter does not assume that every receivable was transferred. It does not assume that every receivable was retained. Both positions require proof, not assumption.

---

## 22.2 The Legal Issue

The legal issue can be stated precisely.

A national bank that operates a credit-card securitization program typically:

1. Files detailed prospectuses and registration statements with the SEC describing the program and its terms.
2. Transfers pools of eligible receivables to a trust on a regular basis — often daily or monthly — pursuant to a Receivables Purchase Agreement and a Transfer and Servicing Agreement.
3. Executes servicing agreements under which it continues to administer accounts on behalf of the trust.
4. Discloses in its annual reports to the SEC the volume, composition, and performance of the receivable pools held by the trust.
5. Takes deductions from taxable income and makes accounting entries reflecting the transfer.
6. Maintains detailed records of accounts transferred, accounts removed, and account eligibility.

These disclosures and practices create an extensive public record. The question is: when a bank with this public record sues to collect a charged-off receivable, and the defendant challenges the bank's present right to enforce, does the existence and comprehensiveness of the routine transfer practice create any evidentiary inference about the specific receivable at issue?

---

## 22.3 Public Securitization Disclosures

### 22.3.1 What the Disclosures Show

Public securitization disclosures — prospectuses, prospectus supplements, Form 10-K annual reports, Form ABS-15G filings, and related materials available on EDGAR — establish several things with reasonable certainty about participating banks:

**Program existence.** The disclosures establish that the bank operates a receivables securitization program. This is not in dispute. The program documents are public, authenticated by their presence on EDGAR, and regularly cross-referenced in subsequent filings.

**Transfer eligibility criteria.** The governing documents define what types of receivables are eligible for transfer. These definitions vary by program but typically include criteria related to account status, minimum balance, and account age. They commonly exclude accounts that are ineligible for assignment under applicable law, accounts where the obligor has filed for bankruptcy, and, in some programs, accounts below a minimum balance threshold.

**Transfer volume.** Annual reports disclose the aggregate principal balance of receivables in the trust, the volume of new receivables added during the period, and the volume removed. These disclosures establish that transfers occur at scale and on a regular basis.

**Transfer mechanics.** The governing agreements describe the process by which eligible receivables are transferred: execution of the RPA, delivery of account schedules, execution of trust certificates, and ongoing maintenance of the trust's receivable pool.

**Removal and reassignment procedures.** The governing agreements describe the conditions under which receivables may be removed from the trust (e.g., charge-off, breach of representations, ineligibility) and the process by which removed receivables are returned.

### 22.3.2 What the Disclosures Do Not Show

The public disclosures do not establish, for any particular account:

- Whether that specific account's receivable was included in a transfer under the program.
- Whether the specific receivable was on any particular account schedule.
- Whether the specific receivable was later removed and reassigned.
- Whether the plaintiff currently holds the specific receivable.

This is the distinction between program-level evidence and account-level evidence described in Chapter 08. The disclosures establish how the program operates. They do not establish what happened to any particular receivable within the program.

## 22.4 Routine Business Practice as an Evidentiary Concept

### 22.4.1 The Routine Practice Doctrine in General

Rules of evidence in many jurisdictions recognize that evidence of a person's or organization's routine practice may be used to prove that the person or organization acted in conformity with that practice on a particular occasion. The Federal Rules of Evidence address this in Rule 406, which provides that evidence of an organization's routine practice is admissible to prove that on a particular occasion the organization acted in conformity with the practice.

Virginia has adopted rules of evidence, but RSAF has not yet verified whether Virginia Rule of Evidence 2:406, or an equivalent provision, expressly recognizes routine practice evidence in the same terms as FRE 406. Research is required. See Section 22.8.

Delaware state courts apply Delaware rules of evidence, and research into Delaware's routine-practice evidence doctrine as applied in civil collection actions is likewise pending.

### 22.4.2 How the Doctrine Might Apply

If governing law recognizes routine practice evidence, the argument available to a plaintiff would proceed as follows:

1. The bank's public disclosures establish that it routinely transferred all eligible receivables to the trust under the governing agreements.
2. The defendant's receivable was an eligible receivable under the program's eligibility criteria.
3. Therefore, the defendant's receivable was transferred — that is, the bank acted in conformity with its routine practice.

This argument has structural plausibility. It is the kind of argument that routine practice evidence is designed to support. The question is whether: (a) the governing law recognizes routine practice evidence in the applicable context; (b) the plaintiff can establish the predicate — that the transfer was, in fact, the routine practice applied to all eligible receivables; and (c) the inference is rebuttable, and if so, how.

### 22.4.3 Competing Considerations

Several considerations complicate the routine practice argument.

**Not all eligible receivables may have been transferred.** Most securitization programs describe categories of ineligible receivables. Even within eligible categories, program administrators sometimes exclude specific accounts. The public disclosures may not establish that every eligible account was transferred without exception.

**The program may have changed over time.** A bank that operated a comprehensive securitization program in one year may have modified, suspended, or wound down the program in a subsequent year. The timing of the account's origination relative to the active period of the program matters.

**Charge-off and removal complicate the inference.** If the routine practice is that eligible receivables are transferred into the trust, and the bank later sues to collect a charged-off account, the inference about the current status of the receivable — who holds it now — requires an additional step. Even if the routine practice supports an inference that the receivable was transferred into the trust, the current plaintiff's right to enforce requires proof that the receivable was removed from the trust and reassigned to the plaintiff (or retained without transfer, which would contradict the initial inference).

**Routine practice evidence proves conformity, not outcome.** If a court accepts routine practice evidence, it supports an inference that the act occurred — that the transfer happened. It does not resolve who currently holds the receivable or whether all subsequent steps in the chain were completed.

## 22.5 Structural Evidence and Transaction Evidence

The tension in this analysis returns to the fundamental RSAF distinction between structural evidence and transaction evidence.

The argument from routine practice is, at its core, an argument that structural evidence — the program documents establishing the routine — gives rise to an inference of the transaction-level event: this specific transfer of this specific receivable.

RSAF's existing framework treats structural evidence as defining what proof should exist, not as proof itself. The routine practice argument would, if accepted, modify that framework by allowing structural evidence to raise an inference of the transaction — subject to rebuttal by account-specific evidence.

Whether that modification is supported by governing law is the open question this chapter presents.

The current RSAF position is that, absent clear governing authority recognizing that inference, structural evidence and the documented routine practice of the bank do not alone establish that any specific receivable was transferred. A plaintiff who relies solely on program documents and routine practice to establish ownership has not yet established the transaction-level fact.

## 22.6 Possible Legal Inferences — Identified, Not Adopted

The following possible legal inferences arise from the documented routine securitization practice. They are identified here for research purposes. RSAF does not presently adopt any of them as established legal positions.

**Possible Inference A: The transfer occurred.**

From proof that a bank routinely transferred all eligible receivables, and proof that the defendant's receivable met the eligibility criteria, a court might draw the inference that the specific receivable was transferred.

Status: Open legal question. Depends on whether governing law recognizes routine practice evidence in this context and whether the predicate can be established. See Section 22.8.

**Possible Inference B: The bank did not retain the receivable.**

From proof that the bank's practice was to transfer all eligible receivables, a court might draw the inference that the bank did not retain this specific receivable absent evidence of exclusion.

Status: Open legal question. This framing shifts the analytical burden toward asking whether the bank has shown retention rather than whether the trust has shown transfer. Whether this inference is available depends on governing law and how the burden is allocated.

**Possible Inference C: The receivable was removed from the trust after charge-off.**

From proof that the bank's routine practice was to remove charged-off receivables from the trust under the governing agreements, a court might infer that this charged-off receivable was so removed.

Status: Open legal question. Depends on the same routine practice doctrine analysis and on the specific terms of the governing agreement's removal procedures. Even if inference A were accepted, inference C requires a separate step.

**Possible Inference D: The bank reacquired the receivable after removal.**

From proof that the bank received reassigned receivables from the trust upon removal, a court might infer that this specific receivable was reassigned to the bank.

Status: Open legal question. Requires the same underlying analysis and an additional link in the chain. Even if inferences A and C were both accepted, inference D requires still further proof or inference.

None of these possible inferences is currently adopted by RSAF as a position. Each requires independent research and authority before it could be incorporated into the framework. Each is also subject to rebuttal by account-specific evidence showing that the receivable was not, in fact, treated in the routine manner.

## 22.7 Competing Legal Positions

**Position 1: Program documents alone are legally insufficient to establish a specific transfer.**

Under this position — which reflects RSAF's current analytical default — the existence of a transfer program and the documented routine practice do not prove the specific transfer. The plaintiff must produce account-specific documentary evidence at each step. This position is supported by RSAF's core distinction between structural and transaction evidence, and by the burden principle that the plaintiff must prove every element.

**Position 2: Routine practice evidence creates a rebuttable inference of transfer.**

Under this position, a plaintiff who establishes that its routine practice was to transfer all eligible receivables, and that the defendant's receivable met the eligibility criteria, may invoke a routine practice inference to establish the transfer. The defendant may rebut the inference by producing evidence that the specific receivable was excluded from the program.

This position would require governing law that recognizes routine practice evidence in this context (an open question) and would require the plaintiff to establish the predicate facts about the comprehensiveness and consistency of the practice.

**Position 3: Public disclosures are admissions binding the plaintiff under the evidentiary rules.**

Under this position, the bank's SEC filings are party admissions that its securitization program transferred eligible receivables. The bank cannot contradict those admissions in subsequent litigation. This framing treats the disclosures as binding rather than merely probative, which could support an inference of transfer for eligible accounts.

The scope and applicability of the party-admission doctrine to SEC filings in subsequent consumer collection litigation is an open legal question. The admissions framework also raises the question of which entity's admissions bind the plaintiff — the disclosing entity may not be identical to the suing entity.

**Position 4: The absence of removal documentation shifts the analysis.**

Under this position, if the bank's own program documents require removal documentation when a receivable leaves the trust, and the bank cannot produce that documentation, the bank has failed its own procedural requirements. This does not prove the receivable was never transferred; it may instead prove the bank failed to document the removal properly.

This framing is notable because it is not necessarily favorable to the defendant: if the bank's failure to document removal reflects a failure in its own internal process, the receivable may remain in the trust — making the bank, rather than the plaintiff, the potential wrong party to the lawsuit.

## 22.8 Open Legal Question

**The question.** Whether Virginia law, Delaware law, or applicable federal evidence law recognizes a rebuttable evidentiary inference that a specific credit-card receivable was transferred because the issuing bank routinely transferred eligible receivables pursuant to a publicly disclosed securitization program.

**Why this question matters.** If the answer is yes, the plaintiff may be able to establish the transfer step through routine practice evidence combined with eligibility proof, without producing an account-specific transfer schedule. If the answer is no, the plaintiff must produce account-specific documentation regardless of the comprehensiveness of the disclosed program. The answer affects the evidentiary burden on the standing element in a significant way.

**Status.** This question has not been resolved by binding authority in Virginia. RSAF has not identified binding Virginia Supreme Court or Virginia Court of Appeals authority directly addressing whether the routine practice of a securitization issuer creates an evidentiary inference about specific account transfers in consumer collection litigation.

**Research required.** The following sources should be reviewed before RSAF adopts a position:

- Virginia Rule of Evidence 2:406 (routine practice), or the equivalent provision, including its scope, predicate requirements, and application in commercial contexts.
- Virginia case law applying routine practice evidence, including any consumer financial context.
- Delaware Rule of Evidence 406, if applicable, and Delaware case law.
- Federal Rule of Evidence 406 and federal case law applying it in the securitization context.
- Bankruptcy opinions addressing the sufficiency of transfer documentation in consumer receivables portfolios, which may address the routine practice argument in analogous contexts.
- Accounting standards governing the treatment of securitization transfers — particularly ASC 860 (formerly SFAS 140/166/167) — to understand what the bank's own accounting treatment implies about the nature and completeness of the transfers.
- OCC guidance on national bank asset sales and securitization, which may address documentary standards.
- FDIC Safe Harbor Rule (12 C.F.R. § 360.6) and any guidance on the documentation requirements for securitization transfers to qualify for true-sale treatment.
- SEC Regulation AB (17 C.F.R. Part 229) and SEC guidance on disclosure standards for securitization programs, which may describe what transfers are required to be documented and how.
- Securitization case law from Virginia courts, the Fourth Circuit, and other circuits addressing the sufficiency of proof of transfer in consumer receivable litigation.

**Current RSAF position.** Pending completion of this research, RSAF treats public securitization documents as structural evidence defining the plaintiff's own transfer framework. RSAF does not presently treat those documents alone as proof that a specific receivable was transferred. Whether governing law permits a broader evidentiary inference remains an open legal question requiring additional research. See also OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md.

## 22.9 Evidentiary Significance for Active Cases

Until the open legal question is resolved, RSAF must handle cases in which the plaintiff relies on routine practice arguments as follows.

**Acknowledge the documented routine.** RSAF does not deny that national bank securitization programs routinely transfer eligible receivables. The public disclosures establish the program. The argument that the program is irrelevant or fabricated is not credible.

**Require account-specific documentation.** RSAF maintains that the existence of the routine does not substitute for account-specific proof. The question of whether governing law provides a different answer is one RSAF identifies as open, not one it resolves in the plaintiff's favor.

**Preserve the argument.** Where a defendant challenges standing, the argument that the plaintiff must produce account-specific documentation — even in the face of routine practice evidence — should be preserved at every procedural stage. If the open legal question is later resolved in favor of a routine practice inference, account-specific documentation evidence is still the most reliable counter-evidence.

**Do not overstate RSAF's position.** RSAF must not say that routine securitization practice can never support an inference of specific transfer. That is an assertion about settled law that RSAF is not in a position to make. The correct statement is that governing law on this question has not been established, and RSAF currently requires account-specific documentation on the standing element.

## 22.10 What This Chapter Does Not Do

This chapter does not conclude that the plaintiff lacks standing because of the routine securitization argument.

This chapter does not conclude that the plaintiff has standing because of the routine securitization argument.

This chapter identifies a legal question that is material to the evidentiary analysis and explains why additional research is required before RSAF can take a definitive position on it.

That is the purpose of this chapter.

## Judge's Checklist

**When a plaintiff invokes routine securitization practice:**

- [ ] Is the plaintiff arguing that its documented routine practice of transferring eligible receivables creates an inference that this specific receivable was transferred?
- [ ] Whether that inference is legally recognized under Virginia, Delaware, or federal evidence law is currently an open question. See OPEN-LEGAL-QUESTIONS.md (OLQ-001).

**Questions to ask the plaintiff:**

- [ ] What evidence establishes that this specific receivable met the eligibility criteria for transfer under the program?
- [ ] What account-specific documentation shows this receivable was actually included in a transfer — not merely that the program transfers eligible receivables?
- [ ] If the program transferred the receivable into the trust, what account-specific documentation shows it was later removed and reassigned to the plaintiff?
- [ ] Is the plaintiff arguing that routine practice eliminates the need for account-specific proof, or that routine practice supplements such proof?

**Current RSAF default:**

Program-level evidence establishes the plaintiff's own transfer framework. RSAF does not currently treat that evidence alone as proof that the defendant's specific receivable was transferred. This default applies unless and until binding authority recognizes a broader inference.

**If the court is inclined to accept a routine practice inference:**

- [ ] What predicate must the plaintiff establish to invoke the inference (comprehensiveness of the program, consistent application to all eligible receivables, etc.)?
- [ ] Is the inference rebuttable? What evidence would rebut it?
- [ ] Does the inference, if accepted, resolve the full chain of title — or only one step of it?
