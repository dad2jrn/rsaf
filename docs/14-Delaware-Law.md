# 14 — Delaware Law

## 14.1 Scope and Research Rule

This chapter covers Delaware law as it applies to consumer credit-card receivable litigation when the governing cardmember agreement or securitization documents select Delaware law as controlling.

**Research Rule.** This chapter identifies governing frameworks and key statutes. Before citing any Delaware statute, rule, or case in a court-facing document, verify the current text, the exact citation, and the precise holding. Delaware law is subject to change. This chapter is a research guide, not a primary source.

---

## 14.2 Why Delaware Law Appears in Virginia Litigation

Many national bank credit-card programs are issued by banks chartered under federal law or Delaware state law. Their cardmember agreements commonly include choice-of-law provisions designating Delaware law as controlling for contract interpretation and enforcement.

Virginia courts generally enforce choice-of-law provisions in commercial contracts where the parties have a reasonable basis for selecting the designated state's law, the chosen state has a substantial relationship to the parties or the transaction, and the chosen law does not violate a fundamental public policy of Virginia.

Where Delaware law governs, RSAF must identify the specific Delaware provision, statute, or principle that controls each legal issue before applying it.

---

## 14.3 Delaware UCC Article 9: Sales of Accounts

Delaware has adopted Article 9 of the Uniform Commercial Code at 6 Del. C. § 9-101 et seq.

**Scope.** 6 Del. C. § 9-109 brings within Article 9's coverage the sale of accounts, chattel paper, payment intangibles, and promissory notes. Credit-card receivables are "accounts" under this provision. The sale of credit-card receivables in Delaware-governed transactions falls within Article 9.

**Significance of Article 9 scope.** The fact that Article 9 governs the sale of accounts does not mean that perfection steps are required for the sale to be effective between the parties. Article 9 governs perfection for priority purposes — establishing the seller's interest against third parties, including the seller's trustee in bankruptcy. Between the originating bank and the purchasing transferor, the sale is effective upon execution of the transfer documents regardless of perfection.

**Account debtor's rights.** 6 Del. C. § 9-406 addresses the account debtor's ability to seek proof of assignment before the account debtor is obligated to pay a new creditor. Where the plaintiff has acquired the receivable through assignment, the defendant may invoke this right and the plaintiff must respond with adequate proof.

**Verify:** Before citing any specific Delaware Code section, verify the current text against the Delaware Code as in effect at the relevant time.

## 14.4 Delaware Asset-Backed Securities Facilitation Act

Delaware has enacted legislation intended to facilitate asset-backed securitization by clarifying the treatment of transfers of receivables into securitization structures and confirming that such transfers will be treated as true sales rather than secured transactions under Delaware law.

The Delaware Asset-Backed Securities Facilitation Act is codified at Title 6, Chapter 27A of the Delaware Code (6 Del. C. §§ 2701A et seq.).

RSAF identifies this statute as a potential source of authority on the true-sale characterization of transfers in Delaware-law-governed programs. Before citing this statute, verify:

- the specific provisions relevant to credit-card receivable transfers;
- whether the statute applies to the program and transfer structure at issue;
- any judicial interpretation of the statute's scope or effect.

RSAF must not overstate the statute's effect. Confirming true-sale treatment under state law does not automatically resolve federal tax or bankruptcy treatment, nor does it establish that account-specific documentation requirements were satisfied.

## 14.5 Delaware Contract Interpretation

Delaware applies settled contract interpretation principles:

- Delaware courts give effect to the unambiguous language of a contract.
- Where contract language is unambiguous, the court interprets the contract from the document itself without reference to extrinsic evidence.
- Where contract language is ambiguous, extrinsic evidence of intent may be considered.
- Delaware courts enforce clear and unequivocal contractual limitations on assignment, transfer, or enforcement rights.

In the credit-card context, the cardmember agreement and the securitization program documents are the operative contracts. RSAF must read each document for its actual terms and must not assume that Delaware courts would imply transfer rights that are not expressed.

## 14.6 Choice-of-Law Limits

Delaware law governs matters that the choice-of-law provision assigns to it. Not every issue in a Virginia collection action is governed by Delaware law.

**Procedural law** is governed by Virginia as the forum state, regardless of what the cardmember agreement says.

**Evidence rules** are governed by the forum state's rules (Virginia).

**Limitations periods** may be governed by Virginia law depending on which state's period the Virginia court applies in light of the choice-of-law clause and any Virginia borrowing statute.

**Burden of proof** is a procedural matter governed by the forum state.

RSAF must not assume that Delaware law governs every issue simply because the cardmember agreement designates Delaware law. The choice-of-law analysis must be performed issue by issue.
