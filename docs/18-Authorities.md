# Table of Authorities — Chain of Title in Debt-Buyer Actions

**Subject:** Binding Virginia authority supporting a chain-of-title / proof-of-ownership challenge to a debt buyer's suit, as framed in *Green v. Portfolio Recovery Associates, LLC*, Record No. 0144-22-3 (Va. Ct. App. Dec. 17, 2024) (en banc).

> **Caveats — read first.** This is a research aid, not legal advice; I am not a lawyer. Several authorities below date from 1808–1924. They are cited approvingly by a 2024 published opinion, which is good evidence they remain valid, but **KeyCite/Shepardize each before relying on it in a filing.** Note also the most important practical lesson from *Green*: the en banc majority treated proof of ownership as a **merits / sufficiency-of-the-evidence** question, not a "standing" question, and a pure standing framing was defeated at the summary-judgment gate even on weak evidence. Preserve the issue as **failure of proof on ownership at trial**, and assign error in those terms (or in both terms, distinctly).

---

## How these authorities support a chain-of-title challenge — summary

Taken together, these authorities establish a single, coherent rule for any party — including a debt buyer — that sues to collect a debt it claims to own by assignment: **it must prove an unbroken chain of title to the *specific* debt at issue, link by link, and it carries the burden of doing so.** The burden-of-proof cases (*Tennent's Heirs*, *Lambert*) place the obligation on the assignee. The specific-debt cases (*Lewis's Ex'r*, *Green v. Ashby*) require that the proof identify *this* debtor's *this* account, not a bulk portfolio — a scattered "jumble" of unattached bills of sale proves no transfer. The assignment-mechanics cases (*Union Recovery*, *Cemetery Consultants*, *Cottrell*, *APAC*) confirm that only a party or privy to the contract may sue, so the assignee must trace its title back to the original creditor-debtor agreement. The standing cases (*Morgan*, *Anders Larsen*, *Cupp*, *Mattaponi*) supply the "fairly traceable, particularized, personal stake" overlay — useful if the gap can be framed as the plaintiff having sued the wrong person. The evidence rules and cases (*Va. R. Evid. 2:602, 2:803(6), 2:902(6)*, *Bowman*, *Brown*, *Bradshaw*, *Code § 8.01-32*) defeat the device debt buyers rely on most — a custodian-of-records affidavit that vouches for transfers between prior banks the affiant never worked for, or that substitutes for a missing original agreement. Each link in the chain must, at minimum, show the debtor's name and account number; a custodian can authenticate only the transfer they have personal knowledge of. The result: a debt buyer that produces only bulk bills of sale, no account-level attachments, mismatched account numbers, and a records custodian without personal knowledge of the earlier assignments has not proven chain of title.

## 1. The assignee bears the burden of proving the assignment

### *Tennent's Heirs v. Pattons*, 33 Va. (6 Leigh) 196, 207 (1835)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Tennent%27s+Heirs+v+Pattons+6+Leigh+196)

**Supports:** The foundational burden-allocation rule. A party suing as an assignee must prove the assignment occurred; where the assignment is put in issue and the record contains no proof of it, the failure is fatal to the claim. This places the burden squarely on the debt buyer to prove it owns the debt.

### *Lambert v. Barker*, 232 Va. 21, 25 (1986)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=232+Va+21+Lambert+v+Barker)

**Supports:** Whoever seeks to collect on an account bears the burden of showing both that it owns the account and that it has express or apparent authority to receive payment. Direct, modern Virginia authority that the collector — not the debtor — must make the ownership showing.

## 2. Ownership must be of the *specific* debt — an unbroken chain

### *Lewis's Ex'r v. Bacon's Legatee*, 13 Va. (3 Hen. & M.) 89, 114 (1808)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Lewis+Executor+v+Bacon+3+Hen+M+89)

**Supports:** To recover a debt from a purported debtor, the plaintiff must prove ownership of the *specific* debt at issue. This is the anchor for the rule that a bulk transfer of unspecified "accounts" is not enough — the proof must reach the individual account being sued on.

### *Green v. Ashby*, 33 Va. (6 Leigh) 135, 144–45 (1835)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Green+v+Ashby+6+Leigh+135)

**Supports:** The closest factual analogue. Scattered and ambiguous evidence — bills, an unintelligible ("mutilated") paper, and testimony from a witness who never saw an assignment — proves no transfer of the debt and cannot create the privity necessary to support the action. Directly supports attacking a debt buyer's pile of unattached bills of sale and conclusory affidavits as proving nothing.

## 3. An assignee stands in the assignor's shoes — and only a party/privy may sue

### *Union Recovery Ltd. P'ship v. Horton*, 252 Va. 418, 423 (1996)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=252+Va+418+Union+Recovery+v+Horton)

**Supports:** An assignee "stands in the shoes" of the assignor and acquires all of the assignor's rights and remedies — but no more. If the chain is broken at any link, the purported assignee never stepped into the original creditor's shoes and has nothing to enforce.

### *Cemetery Consultants, Inc. v. Tidewater Funeral Dirs. Assocs.*, 219 Va. 1001, 1003 (1979)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=219+Va+1001+Cemetery+Consultants)

**Supports:** An action on a contract must be brought by the party in whom the legal interest is vested — ordinarily only the promisee, the promisor, or their privies. A debt buyer must prove it became a privy through a complete chain of valid assignments; otherwise it is a legal stranger to the contract.

### *Cottrell v. General Sys. Software Corp.*, 248 Va. 401, 403 (1994)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=248+Va+401+Cottrell+v+General+Systems+Software)

**Supports:** Reaffirms the party-or-privy requirement for suit on a contract. Corroborating authority for *Cemetery Consultants*.

### *APAC-Virginia, Inc. v. Va. Dep't of Highways & Transp.*, 9 Va. App. 450, 452 (1990)
[Court of Appeals of Virginia, published] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=9+Va+App+450+APAC+Virginia)

**Supports:** Court of Appeals application of the party-or-privy rule, useful as on-point intermediate-court authority in a Court of Appeals appeal.

## 4. Standing requires a fairly-traceable, particularized, personal stake

### *Morgan v. Bd. of Supervisors of Hanover Cnty.*, 302 Va. 46 (2023)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Justia)](https://law.justia.com/cases/virginia/supreme-court/2023/211021.html) · [Official PDF (vacourts.gov)](https://www.vacourts.gov/static/opinions/opnscvwp/1211021.pdf)

**Supports:** The current lead standing case, and the fulcrum of *Green*. Standing concerns the characteristics of the party who files suit and asks whether the claimant has a personal stake — a direct, immediate, pecuniary, and substantial interest in the decision. **Double-edged:** the *Green* majority used *Morgan* to hold that ownership is a merits question (not standing), while the *Green* dissent used *Morgan*'s "fairly traceable" language to argue the injury must be traceable to *this* defendant. Cite with awareness of both edges.

### *Anders Larsen Tr. v. Bd. of Supervisors of Fairfax Cnty.*, 301 Va. 116, 120–21 (2022)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=301+Va+116+Anders+Larsen+Trust)

**Supports:** Source of the "direct, immediate, pecuniary, and substantial interest" formulation and the principle that standing turns on the characteristics of the party suing. Supports the argument that a plaintiff who cannot show it owns the account lacks the requisite interest.

### *Cupp v. Bd. of Supervisors*, 227 Va. 580, 589 (1984)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=227+Va+580+Cupp+v+Board+of+Supervisors)

**Supports:** The point of standing is to ensure the person asserting a position has a substantial legal right to do so and that his rights will be affected by the disposition. Supports the framing that a debt buyer asserting a position with no proven legal right to the account lacks standing.

### *Mattaponi Indian Tribe v. Commonwealth*, 261 Va. 366, 376 (2001)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=261+Va+366+Mattaponi+Indian+Tribe)

**Supports:** The injury must be "fairly traceable" to the challenged action of the defendant, not the result of independent action by a third party. The doctrinal hook for "if you sued the wrong person, you lack standing" — i.e., the alleged unpaid debt must be traceable to *this* debtor's account, not some other account-holder's.

## 5. Evidence rules and cases that defeat conclusory custodian affidavits

### Va. R. Evid. 2:602 — Personal knowledge
[Rule of the Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Official rules text](https://www.google.com/search?q=Virginia+Rule+of+Evidence+2:602+personal+knowledge)

**Supports:** A witness may testify only to matters of which they have personal knowledge. A debt buyer's records custodian cannot have personal knowledge of transfers between prior banks they never worked for, so their testimony about earlier links in the chain is incompetent.

### Va. R. Evid. 2:803(6) and 2:902(6) — Business records; self-authentication
[Rules of the Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Official rules text](https://www.google.com/search?q=Virginia+Rule+of+Evidence+2:803+2:902+business+records)

**Supports:** The business-records hearsay exception and self-authentication require a showing that someone with personal knowledge produced a reliable record in the ordinary course of business. Bulk bills of sale and stray spreadsheets lacking that foundation do not qualify, and a custodian cannot bootstrap records created by upstream banks.

### *Bowman v. Commonwealth*, 28 Va. App. 204, 210–11 (1998)
[Court of Appeals of Virginia, published] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=28+Va+App+204+Bowman+v+Commonwealth)

**Supports:** A witness whose statement rests on a review of records *not in evidence* lacks personal knowledge, and such testimony is hearsay. The case that sinks a custodian's attempt to vouch for the existence and content of upstream assignments they only "reviewed."

### *Brown v. Commonwealth*, 54 Va. App. 107, 115 (2009)
[Court of Appeals of Virginia, published] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=54+Va+App+107+Brown+v+Commonwealth+best+evidence)

**Supports:** Best-evidence rule. Where the contents of a writing are to be proved, the writing itself must be produced or its absence sufficiently accounted for. Supports demanding the actual assignment documents and underlying agreement, not summaries.

### *Bradshaw v. Commonwealth*, 16 Va. App. 374, 379 (1993)
[Court of Appeals of Virginia, published] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=16+Va+App+374+Bradshaw+v+Commonwealth+best+evidence)

**Supports:** Companion best-evidence authority quoted in *Brown*. Corroborates the requirement to produce the original writing or properly account for its absence.

### Va. Code § 8.01-32 — Action on lost evidences of debt
[Code of Virginia] · [Official text (law.lis.virginia.gov)](https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title8.01/chapter3/section8.01-32/)

**Supports:** If the original written evidence of the debt is unavailable, the plaintiff may sue on it *only if* it verifies under oath, in open court or by affidavit, that the document was lost or destroyed. A debt buyer cannot simply skip the underlying agreement; failure to follow this procedure is an evidentiary gap in proving the debt (and, by extension, what was actually assigned).

## 6. Account-stated theory (the debt buyer's common alternative basis)

### *Ellison v. Weintrob*, 139 Va. 29, 35 (1924)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=139+Va+29+Ellison+v+Weintrob)

**Supports:** An account stated requires proof that the accounts between the parties were actually settled, or are presumed settled because one party retained the other's account for a long time without objection. Forces the plaintiff to prove a real settled account between *these* parties, not a number on a spreadsheet.

### *Radford v. Fowlkes*, 85 Va. 820, 852 (1889)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=85+Va+820+Radford+v+Fowlkes+account+stated)

**Supports:** Corroborating account-stated authority — relevant evidence includes a final, definite balance and statements sent to and retained by the debtor without timely objection.

### *Watson v. Lyle's Adm'r*, 31 Va. (4 Leigh) 236, 249 (1833)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=Watson+v+Lyle+4+Leigh+236+account+stated)

**Supports:** Early source of the account-stated standard quoted in *Ellison*. Useful for the proposition's pedigree.

## 7. Breach-of-contract elements (the merits, if reached)

### *Navar, Inc. v. Fed. Bus. Council*, 291 Va. 338, 344 (2016)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=291+Va+338+Navar+v+Federal+Business+Council)

**Supports:** The elements of breach of contract — a legally enforceable obligation, the defendant's breach, and resulting injury or damage to the plaintiff. Frames what a debt buyer must prove *beyond* ownership to win on the merits.

### *Ulloa v. QSP, Inc.*, 271 Va. 72, 79 (2006)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=271+Va+72+Ulloa+v+QSP)

**Supports:** Source of the breach-of-contract elements quoted in *Navar*.

### *Sunrise Continuing Care, LLC v. Wright*, 277 Va. 148, 156 (2009)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=277+Va+148+Sunrise+Continuing+Care+v+Wright)

**Supports:** Proof of damages is an essential element of a breach-of-contract claim, and failure to prove it warrants dismissal. Supports attacking a debt buyer that cannot prove the account is actually unpaid or the amount owed.

## 8. Procedural posture and standard of review (why framing matters)

### *Klaiber v. Freemason Assocs.*, 266 Va. 478, 484 (2003)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=266+Va+478+Klaiber+v+Freemason+Associates)

**Supports:** Summary judgment is a drastic remedy, appropriate only where no material facts are genuinely in dispute; forced or strained inferences will not defeat it. This is the standard that let PRA's thin evidence survive Green's *pre-trial* standing motion — the reason to press the ownership failure *at and after trial*, not only on summary judgment.

### *Fultz v. Delhaize Am., Inc.*, 278 Va. 84, 88 (2009)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=278+Va+84+Fultz+v+Delhaize)

**Supports:** Summary judgment is improper where the evidence is conflicting on a material point or reasonable persons could draw different conclusions. Companion to *Klaiber*; explains why a court may decline to resolve ownership at the summary-judgment stage.

### *Platt v. Griffith*, 299 Va. 690, 692 (2021)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=299+Va+690+Platt+v+Griffith)

**Supports:** De novo appellate review of standing. Establishes the standard if the ownership gap is framed and reviewed as a standing question.

### *Malbrough v. Commonwealth*, 275 Va. 163, 168 (2008)
[Supreme Court of Virginia] · [Full text (Google Scholar)](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=275+Va+163+Malbrough+v+Commonwealth)

**Supports:** A trial court's factual findings are binding on appeal unless plainly wrong or without evidence to support them. The lens the *Green* dissent used to argue the circuit court was "plainly wrong" to find PRA proved ownership — the standard of review if the issue is framed as a merits/sufficiency finding.

## Primary case under review

### *Green v. Portfolio Recovery Associates, LLC*, Record No. 0144-22-3 (Va. Ct. App. Dec. 17, 2024) (en banc)
[Official opinion PDF (vacourts.gov)](https://www.vacourts.gov/static/opinions/opncavwp/0144223.pdf)

Panel opinion (vacated on rehearing en banc): *Green v. Portfolio Recovery Assocs., LLC*, 80 Va. App. 119 (2024) · [Justia](https://law.justia.com/cases/virginia/court-of-appeals-published/2024/0144-22-3.html)

**Note on weight:** The en banc majority is binding but did **not** reach the merits of chain of title; it held PRA's evidence sufficient to survive a pre-trial standing challenge and cabined the appeal to "standing." The thorough chain-of-title analysis is in **Judge Causey's en banc dissent** (joined by Chaney) and in the **vacated panel opinion** — both persuasive only. Build the affirmative argument from the binding underlying authorities listed above rather than from the dissent itself.
