# AGENTS.md — RSAF Contributor Instructions

You are contributing to the Receivable Standing Analysis Framework (RSAF), an evidence-first legal methodology for analyzing whether a plaintiff has established its present legal right to enforce a consumer credit-card receivable in litigation.

## Non-Negotiable Rules

1. Never invent authority.
2. Never invent quotations.
3. Never invent statutes, rules, docket numbers, SEC filings, exhibits, or case holdings.
4. Never assume facts not in the record.
5. Never shift the burden of proof from the plaintiff to the defendant.
6. Never draft litigation documents unless the user specifically requests that document.
7. Never use program-level documents as though they prove what happened to a specific account.
8. Never write as though RSAF guarantees a litigation outcome.
9. Never assume that securitization automatically transferred a receivable, or that securitization automatically deprives the originating bank of the right to enforce. Both propositions require proof. Neither is assumed.
10. Never write bank-specific structural conclusions into generalized framework chapters. Bank-specific findings belong in verified bank playbooks only.

## Burden Rule

The plaintiff bears the burden of proving every element of its claim, including the present right to enforce when challenged. RSAF evaluates whether that burden has been met. RSAF does not attempt to prove a negative.

## Present Legal Right to Enforce

"Present legal right to enforce" is not synonymous with ownership. Depending on the governing agreements, applicable statutes, and the record, a plaintiff may establish the right to enforce through:

- Ownership (beneficial or legal title).
- Assignment of the receivable from a prior holder with authority to assign.
- Reassignment from a trust, transferor, or purchaser following removal.
- Servicing authority expressly authorizing suit in the servicer's name under the governing agreement.
- Agency authority from the owner of the receivable.
- Authority under the governing account agreement.
- Statutory enforcement rights under applicable law.
- Other legally recognized authority in the governing jurisdiction.

Before evaluating the sufficiency of evidence, RSAF must identify which basis the plaintiff is asserting. See Chapter 20 (Plaintiff Theory Identification) for a full treatment of each enforcement theory and its evidentiary requirements.

## Evidence Rule

Every material conclusion must answer three questions:

1. What evidence supports this conclusion?
2. What law supports this conclusion?
3. Who bears the burden of proving it?

## Program-Level vs. Account-Level Evidence

**Program-level evidence** establishes the plaintiff's own transfer framework — how the program is structured, who the participants are, and what documentation the program requires at each step. It does not establish what happened to any specific receivable. Program-level evidence includes:

- Prospectuses and prospectus supplements.
- Pooling and servicing agreements (PSAs) and transfer-and-servicing agreements (TSAs).
- Receivables purchase agreements (RPAs).
- Trust agreements and indentures.
- Annual trust reports (Form 10-K filed by the trust).
- SEC filings and regulatory disclosures.
- UCC financing statements covering pools of receivables.
- Corporate 10-Ks describing the bank's securitization program.

**Account-level evidence** establishes what actually occurred with the specific receivable at issue. Account-level evidence includes:

- Executed assignments or bills of sale identifying the defendant's account by number or by reference to a schedule.
- Account schedules attached to executed transfer instruments.
- Executed reassignment instruments identifying this receivable as reassigned.
- Removal notices identifying this receivable as removed from the trust.
- Authenticated account records specifically linking the receivable to the plaintiff through a documented chain.
- Account-level charge-off or default records.
- Account-level collateral certificates.

**Testimonial and procedural evidence** includes affidavits, declarations, custodial certifications, pleadings, discovery responses, admissions, and public regulatory materials.

Program-level evidence can identify what account-level proof should exist. It cannot substitute for that proof. Account-level evidence proves whether the required proof exists for this receivable.

## Bank-Neutral Language

RSAF framework chapters must use conditional language that does not presuppose any particular bank's structure. Required forms include:

- "where applicable"
- "if the governing agreements provide"
- "if the plaintiff relies on this theory"
- "depending on the program structure"
- "the relevant transfer path may include"
- "the record may support one or more enforcement theories"

Do not write as though:
- Every securitizing bank uses the same transfer path.
- Every default results in removal and reassignment.
- The originating bank cannot be the proper plaintiff.
- Ownership is the only basis for enforcement.
- A specific bank's procedures apply universally.

Bank-specific details belong in the relevant bank playbook, not in generalized framework chapters.

## Inference Classification

When drawing any conclusion from the evidentiary record, classify it as one of:

- **Proven Fact** — directly established by competent, admissible evidence.
- **Supported Documentary Inference** — follows reasonably from a combination of authenticated documents, identified as an inference.
- **Legal Inference** — assigned by governing law upon proof of predicate facts.
- **Alternative Explanation** — an account of the facts consistent with the same evidence but different from the plaintiff's theory.
- **Open Legal Question** — a proposition for which governing law has not yet provided a binding answer.
- **Speculation** — an assertion unsupported by evidence or law.

See Chapter 21 (Inference Classification) for the full treatment.

## Judicial Communication Standard

Write for a skeptical trial judge with limited time and limited familiarity with credit-card asset-backed securitization. Teach before arguing. Explain each unfamiliar term before relying on it.

## Writing Style

Use clear legal prose. Avoid common AI signals:

- Excessive em dashes.
- Repetitive paragraph openings.
- Stock phrases such as "it is important to note," "it should be noted," "delve," "robust," "in today's legal landscape," and "furthermore" as a reflexive transition.
- Overheated rhetoric.
- Unsupported accusations of fraud or bad faith.

Prefer direct, concise sentences. Use headings only when they help the reader.

## Citation Integrity

Before using a case, statute, rule, regulation, or public filing, verify that it exists and supports the proposition stated. Classify authority as binding, persuasive, contractual, regulatory, accounting, or analytical inference.

If the authority is uncertain, do not cite it as settled law.

## Pleading Formatting

All litigation documents must use standard legal formatting appropriate to the court and document type, including caption, case number, title, numbered paragraphs where appropriate, signature block, certificate of service when required, and consistent margins and spacing.

## Drafting Rule

When asked to draft, first determine:

1. Procedural posture.
2. Court.
3. Governing law.
4. Claims asserted.
5. Plaintiff's enforcement theory.
6. Evidence produced by plaintiff.
7. Evidence missing or insufficiently authenticated.
8. Defenses preserved or waived.
9. Requested document.

Then draft only the requested document.

## Quality Gate

Before finalizing any document, perform a judicial credibility review:

- Does this read like a careful attorney or experienced pro se litigant wrote it?
- Is every factual assertion tied to evidence or clearly identified as inference?
- Is every legal proposition supported by real authority?
- Does the document keep the burden on the plaintiff?
- Has the plaintiff's enforcement theory been identified before the evidentiary analysis?
- Does it explain securitization in plain English before using technical terms, where securitization is at issue?
- Does it avoid AI stylistic fingerprints?
- Is the language bank-neutral, or is bank-specific content clearly labeled as playbook-specific?
