# Glossary

This glossary defines terms that appear throughout the RSAF framework. These definitions govern how terms are used within RSAF. Terms that are commonly confused — or that are used inconsistently in litigation practice — are distinguished explicitly.

For a judge reading a collection case: the key distinction to hold in mind is between the *servicer* (who manages the account) and the *owner* (who holds the right to be paid). A bank may be doing both, or only one.

---

## Terms and Definitions

---

### Account

The credit relationship between an issuing bank and a cardholder. The account encompasses the governing agreement (cardmember agreement), the credit facility, and the administrative relationship that produces statements and payment processing.

The account is distinct from the receivable. See *Receivable*.

**RSAF use:** "Account" refers to the ongoing credit relationship. "Receivable" refers to the specific right to payment. Do not use these interchangeably.

**See:** Chapter 09 (§ 9.3)

---

### Account Debtor

The person obligated to make payment on a receivable. In consumer credit-card collection litigation, the defendant is the account debtor.

**RSAF use:** Neutral term for the cardholder in their capacity as the party from whom payment is sought.

---

### Agency

A legal relationship in which one party (the agent) acts on behalf of another (the principal). An agent may have authority to perform acts — including commencing litigation — on the principal's behalf without owning the underlying asset.

**Distinguish from ownership:** A servicer that collects payments for a trust is an agent of the trust, not an owner of the receivables. An agent's authority to act is governed by the principal's instructions and the governing agreement, not by ownership.

**RSAF use:** Where a plaintiff asserts authority as agent of the trust or another entity, it must identify the principal, the governing agreement authorizing the agency, and the scope of that authority. Agency must be documented, not assumed.

---

### Assignee

A person or entity that has received an assignment — a transfer of a right from an assignor. In receivable litigation, the assignee is the entity to whom the receivable has been transferred.

**Distinguish from holder:** An assignee is one category of holder. "Holder" is the broader term encompassing any person with the present right to enforce; "assignee" specifically denotes acquisition through a transfer from a prior holder.

**RSAF use:** Where the plaintiff claims to be an assignee, it must produce account-specific documentation of the assignment — identifying the specific receivable, the assignor, and the assignee.

---

### Authentication

The process of establishing that a document is what it purports to be. Authentication is a threshold requirement for admissibility but does not establish the truth of the document's contents.

**RSAF use:** Authentication answers "Is this document what it claims to be?" It does not answer "Does this document prove the plaintiff owns the receivable?" These are separate questions requiring separate evidence.

**See:** Chapter 07 (§ 7.7), Chapter 17

---

### Beneficial Ownership

An interest in the economic benefits of property without holding legal title. In a securitization trust, investors typically hold *beneficial interests* in the trust — they receive the cash flows — while the *owner trustee* holds legal title on the trust's behalf.

**Distinguish from legal title:** Legal title is held by the owner trustee. Beneficial ownership is held by the trust beneficiaries (investors). For purposes of enforcement — including the right to sue — the entity holding legal title or the entity expressly authorized by the governing agreements to sue must be the plaintiff, not a beneficial owner alone.

**RSAF use:** Beneficial ownership alone does not establish the present legal right to enforce against a specific account debtor. A plaintiff asserting enforcement rights based on beneficial ownership must identify the governing agreement provision that confers that authority.

---

### Chain of Title

The sequence of documented transfers linking the original holder of a right to its present claimant. For a credit-card receivable, a complete chain requires account-specific evidence at each link.

**RSAF use:** A chain of title has two levels: the structural chain (how the program describes the transfer path) and the account-specific chain (what documents prove this receivable traveled through that path). Only the account-specific chain establishes the plaintiff's present right to enforce. See *Program-Level Evidence*, *Account-Level Evidence*.

**See:** Chapter 16

---

### Holder

A person or entity with the present right to enforce an obligation. The term is broader than "assignee" because it encompasses original creditors, assignees, trustees, and any other party currently entitled to enforce.

**Distinguish from servicer:** A servicer manages an account and may have authority to collect payments, but is not necessarily the holder of the receivable. Servicing rights and enforcement rights are distinct.

**RSAF use:** "Holder" is the functional term for who may enforce the obligation. RSAF uses "present legal right to enforce" as the operative phrase for the standing element. A plaintiff claiming to be the holder must demonstrate that right with account-specific evidence.

---

### Legal Title

The formal ownership interest in property recognized by law, as distinguished from beneficial or equitable ownership. In a securitization trust, the owner trustee holds legal title to the trust assets.

**Distinguish from beneficial ownership:** The owner trustee holds legal title but acts only on instruction. Investors hold beneficial interests. Neither role alone is identical to the right to sue a specific account debtor.

**RSAF use:** When legal title and beneficial ownership are split between different entities, RSAF must identify who holds what interest and which interest authorizes the enforcement action. The governing agreement and applicable law determine which interest carries the right to sue.

---

### Ownership

Shorthand for the present legal right to enforce the specific receivable. In RSAF, "ownership" is not used as a self-executing legal conclusion. It is used as a placeholder for the full question: has the plaintiff demonstrated, through account-specific evidence, that it holds the present legal right to enforce this specific receivable against this specific defendant?

**Do not use interchangeably with:** standing, legal title, beneficial ownership, servicing, or holder. Each of these concepts has a distinct meaning that affects the analysis in a different way.

**RSAF preferred formulation:** "present legal right to enforce" rather than "ownership." This formulation makes explicit that the question is both about the right (legal entitlement) and about enforcement (the right to collect from this defendant at this time).

**See:** Chapter 20 (§ 20.2)

---

### Present Legal Right to Enforce

The plaintiff's current entitlement to collect the specific receivable from the specific defendant. The standing element of the plaintiff's claim.

This is RSAF's operative phrase. It is preferred over "ownership" because it focuses the inquiry on: (1) the specific receivable (not just the plaintiff's general program); (2) the present time (not a historical transfer that may have been unwound); and (3) legal entitlement (not mere custody or servicing authority).

**What the plaintiff must show:** Account-specific, authenticated evidence that it currently holds the right to collect this balance, from this defendant, under the governing law.

**RSAF use:** The standing element in every claim analysis. See Chapter 06 (§ 6.2).

---

### Program-Level Evidence

Documentary evidence about the structure and operation of the plaintiff's securitization or transfer program. Includes prospectuses, governing agreements (RPA, TSA, PSA), trust agreements, SEC filings, and annual reports.

**What it establishes:** How the program is structured; what documentation the governing agreements require at each transfer step; the identities of the participants.

**What it does not establish:** What happened to the defendant's specific receivable; the plaintiff's present legal right to enforce.

**Distinguish from account-level evidence:** Program-level evidence defines the evidentiary framework. Account-level evidence proves what happened within that framework for the specific receivable at issue.

**See:** Chapter 08

---

### Account-Level Evidence

Documentary evidence specifically identifying the defendant's account or receivable and documenting what occurred with it. Includes executed assignments with account schedules listing the defendant's account, removal notices, reassignment instruments, and authenticated records tying the specific receivable to a specific transfer event.

**See:** Chapter 08

---

### Receivable

The specific right to receive payment that arises from charges, interest, fees, and other amounts incurred on a credit-card account. Receivables are property interests that can be transferred, assigned, pledged, and enforced.

**Distinguish from account:** The account is the credit relationship. The receivable is the right to payment arising from that relationship. A bank may continue to administer the account (as servicer) while the receivable has been transferred to a trust.

**See:** Chapter 09 (§ 9.2–9.3)

---

### Servicer

The entity that administers credit-card accounts: sending statements, processing payments, maintaining records, charging off delinquent accounts. Often the originating bank remains as servicer even after transferring receivables to a securitization trust.

**Critical distinction:** Servicing is not ownership. A servicer may generate all the account records — statements, payment history, charge-off records — without holding the right to enforce the receivable. Records generated by a servicer establish what the servicer did; they do not establish who owns the receivable.

**RSAF rule:** Proof that a party is the servicer is not proof that it is the owner. The two roles require separate documentation.

**See:** Chapter 09 (§ 9.8), Chapter 02 (§ 2.2 and § 2.9)

---

### Standing

A party's legal right to maintain a particular action. In receivable litigation, a plaintiff's standing requires it to demonstrate its present legal right to enforce the specific receivable at issue.

**The standing/merits distinction:** Standing may be challenged at the pleading stage as a threshold question (the plaintiff has no right to be in court) or as a failure-of-proof question on the merits (the plaintiff has not proven the ownership element at trial). Both framings are available and should be preserved. A pure standing challenge may fail at summary judgment if a factual dispute exists; a failure-of-proof challenge requires proof at trial.

**Do not confuse with:** ownership (a factual/legal question about who holds the right), legal title (a property concept), or beneficial ownership (an equitable interest).

**See:** Chapter 06 (§ 6.2), Chapter 02 (§ 2.8)

---

### Structural Evidence

Synonymous with program-level evidence. See *Program-Level Evidence*.

---

### Transaction Evidence

Synonymous with account-level evidence. See *Account-Level Evidence*. Used primarily in contrast with structural (program-level) evidence to emphasize that each transfer event requires its own documented proof.

---

## Common Confusions

| Term A | Term B | The Distinction |
|---|---|---|
| Servicer | Owner | Servicer administers; owner holds the right to payment. Records in a servicer's name prove servicing, not ownership. |
| Standing | Ownership | Standing is the procedural question of whether the plaintiff may sue. Ownership is the substantive question of whether it holds the right. They overlap but are not identical. |
| Legal title | Beneficial ownership | Legal title is held by the trustee; beneficial ownership by the investors. Neither alone is the same as the right to sue a specific account debtor. |
| Holder | Assignee | Holder is any party with the right to enforce; assignee is one who received it by transfer. Every assignee is a holder; not every holder is an assignee. |
| Present right to enforce | Historical ownership | The question is who holds the right *now*, not who held it at some prior point. Transfers, assignments, and securitization can alter current ownership. |
| Account-level evidence | Program-level evidence | Account-level proves what happened to *this* receivable. Program-level proves how the program operates generally. |
| Authentication | Proof of ownership | Authentication establishes the document is genuine. It says nothing about what the document proves on the ownership question. |
| Assigned | Owned | A plaintiff may have been assigned rights without fully establishing that the specific receivable is included in the assignment. |
